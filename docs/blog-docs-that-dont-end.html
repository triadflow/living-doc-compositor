A living doc for topics that don't end.

Most living docs are bounded by a piece of work and move toward an outcome. Some topics never finish — a debate, a market, a research frontier. They need a different shape: one where each check-in lands as a delta, not a fresh write-up.

RL
Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedApr 19, 2026 Read9 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-docs-that-dont-end.html

Every living-doc template I'd shipped before this one was bounded by a piece of work that ends: ship a feature, prove a claim, finish a chapter, deep-dive an issue. The work resolves. The doc closes out. The archive of choices made becomes a record. Most knowledge work fits that shape — but not all of it does.

Some topics don't finish. The AI-and-labor debate has been running for a decade and will run for another. Market regimes don't resolve; they shift. A competitive landscape doesn't wrap up; it re-arranges. A research frontier doesn't close; new papers land every month and the center of gravity moves. For any of those, the usual living-doc shape strains. You end up with a one-shot synthesis that's already stale the day you publish it — or you rewrite it every six months from scratch, which is the exact work the living-doc format exists to avoid.

This post is about a new shape that handles those topics, how it composes cleanly on the existing substrate, and what the first instance of it looks like in practice.

What changed in the substrate

The move that makes monitoring legible is simple once you see it: periods become first-class, alongside objective facets. A period is a named check-in window — 2026-H1, 2026-H2, 2027-H1 — and every card carries a lastUpdatedInPeriod marker. The doc has a periods[] array at the root listing the check-in windows and their summaries. Staleness detection flips from "the doc hasn't been touched in N days" to "the current period has closed and the next period note is missing."

That one addition unblocks the whole shape. Once the doc knows about periods, you can render a horizontal timeline at the top showing where the reader is in the history. Every card shows the period it last moved in, so a reader sees at a glance which parts of the doc this check-in touched. A period selector could eventually let you rewind the doc to any prior check-in for a clean before/after — that one's still presentational today, but the data is there.

Alongside periods, four convergence types land:

  • expert-stance-track — sustained public-intellectual stance across many works, with fields for current position, evolution since last period, and a stance-shift flag. Distinct from maintainer-stance, which is for one-off positions on a specific thread.
  • indicator-trace — a tracked numeric metric with a latest-value (as text), an as-of date, a forecast, a delta versus the prior period, and a categorical trend (rising / stable / falling / resolved).
  • citation-feed — dated source entries grouped by the period they were added in. Retroactive additions to a prior period are allowed but flagged.
  • position-cluster-map — two-axis layout where named voices get placed, and prior-period position is preserved per card so movement is inspectable.

Plus a small status set — trend — and a citation-state set for sources. Existing types get reused cleanly: proof-ladder carries the empirical predictions as rungs whose evidence accumulates, and decision-record carries the one-per-period note summarising what shifted.

None of that is exotic. Each addition is one registry entry. The renderer gained a period strip and a small period badge on each card. The monitoring template holds it all together as a reusable starting shape.

The doc is bounded by a topic, not by an outcome. The monitoring shape is the one that makes that distinction first-class.

The first instance: AI and labor

The first doc using the shape is a tracker for five economists on AI and the future of work: AI and Labor: Economist Position Tracker. It's seeded from a one-shot April 2026 synthesis of McAfee, Autor, Acemoglu, Brynjolfsson, and Aghion. Here's what it carries:

  • Five expert cards — each with a current stance, affiliation, one-paragraph core view, what they emphasise, and the sources that ground it. evolutionSinceLastPeriod is "baseline" in this first period; future check-ins fill it with what shifted.
  • Four prediction rungs — the "what to watch in 2026–2027" claims (productivity persists, entry-level AI-exposed employment keeps falling, wage dispersion widens, flexicurity adoption). Each carries a resolution criterion. All four are planned at baseline.
  • Four indicator cards — US TFP growth (rising, +2.7% in 2025), 22–25yo employment in AI-exposed roles (falling, -13% relative), wage premium in AI-exposed CS sector (rising, +16.7% vs. +7.5% national), OECD flexicurity adoption (stable, 0 explicit programs).
  • Five position-map cards — each thinker placed on two axes: magnitude of AI impact × stance on institutional role. Prior position is "baseline" for everyone; next period it becomes the delta anchor.
  • Fifteen citations grouped under 2026-H1 — every NBER paper, Project Syndicate piece, Fed report, Fortune op-ed, and interview that grounds the baseline.
  • One period note for 2026-H1 with the focus list for 2026-H2.

The doc also declares six doc-local invariants: every expert must cite a period source; every prediction must carry a resolution; every indicator must be dated; every source must have a publication date; the period note must exist when the period closes; and the divergence map must cover every tracked voice. Those aren't suggestions — they're the rules the monitoring shape depends on. A sync run that would violate one of them fails its own self-check before emitting a patch.

The sync skill

Every six months, something has to do the archaeology: scan per-thinker publication feeds, notice stance shifts, ingest new sources, check predictions against fresh evidence, update indicators, draft the period note. Doing that manually is a full afternoon. Not doing it is how monitoring docs rot.

The companion skill /ai-labor-sync runs the archaeology. Given a period-start date, it walks each expert's known feeds (Substack, NBER, Project Syndicate, Brookings, Fed papers, major interview outlets) filtered to the period window, proposes stance updates with evolution notes grounded in specific sources, ingests new citations under the current period's group, proposes resolution transitions on prediction rungs, flags indicators whose latest prints have been released, updates the divergence map where stances shifted, and drafts the period note.

It emits a living-doc-ai-patch/v1 patch — the same structured format the Cmd+K palette uses. The human opens the compositor, reviews each proposed change, accepts or declines, and applies. Nothing mutates silently. The skill is the discovery-then-refinement loop applied to longitudinal research tracking, with the same scope guardrail as everywhere else: AI does the archaeology, human does the governance.

What's still honest about the limits

Numeric status is a workaround. The compositor's status model is categorical, so indicator-trace carries the number as text and uses the trend set for direction. If monitoring docs proliferate, a proper numeric-status field is a follow-up — but this works for v1.

The period selector is presentational, not interactive. You see the periods as a horizontal strip; clicking doesn't rewind the whole doc. That's a larger JS task than this pass could justify, and the data is shaped so it's ready when someone wants to build it.

Entity resolution across projections is a real problem. If you track the same real-world entity in several monitoring docs, they won't resolve to one thing unless you teach your sync layer about it. Emil Eifrem flagged this as under-discussed in AI engineering and he's right.

The pattern generalises

The monitoring shape isn't domain-specific. It fits any topic where named voices, empirical predictions, numeric indicators, and periodic evidence accumulation are the structure of what you want to watch. A few shapes I've thought about since shipping this:

  • Market-regime tracker. Named macro thinkers (Dalio, Druckenmiller, El-Erian, Ferguson), predictions about rates / credit / commodities, indicator dashboard (yield curve, spreads, dollar index), divergence map on hawkish ↔ dovish vs. growth ↔ stagflation.
  • Research-frontier watcher. Named researchers in a specific field, predictions about what will be settled, benchmarks as indicators, citation feed of new preprints and papers, divergence map if a debate has axes.
  • Competitive-landscape monitor. Named competitors as expert voices with stated strategy, predictions about launches and bets, indicators for share of voice / revenue / headcount, citations to analyst reports and earnings calls.
  • Policy-debate state of play. Named policymakers, predictions about passage, indicators on vote counts and polls, citation feed of op-eds and floor statements.

Each of those is the same shape. The registry carries the types; the template carries the scaffolding; a domain-specific sync skill carries the archaeology (/market-regime-sync, /competitor-sync, and so on, following the same pattern as /ai-labor-sync). The distinctive scope — your actual topic — is in the doc, not in the code. Shipping the template means the next person who wants to watch a topic gets to start from the structure instead of inventing it.

What this implies for the substrate

Two observations from shipping this.

Most living-doc work is bounded by outcomes, but some is bounded by topics. The registry handles both, because types describe shapes of work, not domain objects (a point I pressed in The registry is a language). expert-stance-track isn't about economists — it's about any situation where public-intellectual positions evolve. indicator-trace isn't about macroeconomic data — it's about any numeric metric you want to watch. The types transfer across domains because the shapes transfer across domains.

Periods are the unit that made monitoring tractable. Without them, every check-in has to re-derive "what changed since last time" from diffs and memory. With them, the answer is a filter on one field. The cost was small — one root field, one per-card marker, a strip of CSS — and the leverage is the whole monitoring shape. That's usually how the high-leverage moves in this substrate look: a small schema extension that unlocks a new class of doc.

Living-doc-compositor is now a substrate that handles outcome-bounded work, topic-bounded monitoring, cross-domain shapes, and a cadence-driven sync pattern. Next is the renderer work to make the period strip actually interactive — rewind the whole doc to a prior period, see the delta in-place. When that lands, the shape becomes what it's trying to be: a surface that accumulates knowledge of a topic across years, one check-in at a time, and stays readable at any point along the way.

Try it: copy the monitoring-tracker template, fill in your own topic, render with node scripts/render-living-doc.mjs docs/your-doc.json. Or read the first real instance at AI and Labor: Economist Position Tracker.
