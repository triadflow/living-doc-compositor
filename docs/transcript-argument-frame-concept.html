Concept prototype

Turn a transcript into an argument frame.

A transcript is linear. The reasoning inside it is not. This living doc concept extracts the claims, assumptions, supporting logic, weak points, and counterarguments so a reader can see the structure of what was said without rereading the whole conversation.

The point is not summary. It is inspectable reasoning.

A good transcript analysis should answer: what is being claimed, what must be true for it to hold, where does the argument depend on a leap, and what would a serious opposing view say?

ClaimWhat is asserted
AssumptionWhat it relies on
ReasoningHow it connects
FlawWhere it may fail
CounterWhat pushes back

Runtime ambition

The static page is the first step. The larger idea is a living doc that forms during a meeting: as live transcription arrives, the doc begins to show claims, themes, factual assertions, and counterarguments before the conversation is over. Voice makes that collaborative: people can correct, accept, challenge, and sharpen the emerging frame without leaving the conversation. The stronger version is grounded in the meeting's existing context: prior living docs, open decisions, known assumptions, and the work the group is already inside.

Why this matters

Most transcript tools compress the words. This one exposes the frame.

Summaries are useful, but they often hide the architecture of the argument. A living doc can keep the original transcript traceable while separating what was said from what was assumed, inferred, contested, or left open.

Narrative

What story is the transcript telling?

Find the implied causal chain and the repeated ideas that make the speaker's account feel coherent.

Logic

What reasoning carries the claim?

Separate evidence, inference, analogy, authority, prediction, and moral framing instead of blending them.

Risk

Where are the weak links?

Surface missing evidence, alternative explanations, category errors, overgeneralization, and unfalsified premises.

Opposition

What would a serious critic say?

Generate plausible counterarguments without pretending every counterargument has equal weight.

New convergence type

transcript-argument-frame

This type is distinct from theme tracking, findings, proof ladders, and documentary claim defensibility. It is a per-claim analytical surface for reasoning inside speech.

Card-grid, one column

One card per claim or reasoning unit

Each card traces back to transcript spans and keeps five things separate: the claim, assumptions, reasoning, vulnerabilities, and counterarguments.

explicit-supported plausible-inference assumption-heavy countered unsupported contradicted
ClaimThe assertion made or implied by the transcript segment.
Transcript spansTimestamped source references that keep the analysis auditable.
ReasoningThe chain connecting evidence, examples, experience, analogy, or prediction.
AssumptionsPremises that must hold for the reasoning to remain valid.
Potential flawsLeaps, missing baselines, selection effects, ambiguity, or overclaiming.
CounterargumentsStrong objections or alternative explanations, ideally tied to source spans or evidence.
Together with other convergences

The argument frame is the center, not the whole document.

A runtime transcript doc should choose surrounding sections based on what appears in the transcript. The new type explains the reasoning; existing convergence types keep the surrounding analytical work cleanly separated.

Rule

Do not collapse claims into themes

A theme can recur across many claims. A claim is something the transcript asks the listener to accept.

Rule

Do not treat facts as proven

If a speaker states a fact, it enters findings or citation feed until checked against external evidence.

Rule

Keep every inference traceable

The card should show whether a conclusion is explicit in the transcript or inferred from nearby turns.

Context grounding

The meeting doc should know what room it walked into.

A transcript-only frame can explain what was said in the meeting. The higher-value version also knows what the meeting was about before anyone started speaking: prior living docs, open threads, decisions, unresolved claims, source material, and the registry of document types the system can safely use.

Context pack

Load the relevant living docs before the meeting

The system can build a small context pack from the local living-doc library: project docs, prior meeting docs, active dossiers, decision records, proof ladders, and source indexes that match the meeting topic.

Grounded inference

Attach new cards to existing structure

A live claim is more valuable when the doc can say: this repeats an existing assumption, updates a known decision, contradicts a prior finding, or creates a new open question under an existing objective.

Access boundary

Use context with provenance and permissions

Cross-doc grounding should never silently blend private material into the meeting artifact. Every imported context signal needs source identity, freshness, and a clear reason it was allowed into the room.

Before the meeting

Find relevant docsUse the living-doc registry and local library manifest to select candidate docs by project, topic, people, and objective.
Extract context facetsPull only the useful frame: current objective, invariants, open decisions, unresolved claims, active evidence gaps.
Declare context scopeShow the participants which docs are being used and which ones are excluded.

During the meeting

Relate speech to prior structure"This sounds like the same assumption as #A7", "this conflicts with the current decision record", "this is a new proof-ladder rung."
Ask grounded questionsThe visible doc can prompt: are we changing the prior decision, adding evidence, or just restating the old concern?
Keep uncertainty explicitWhen context matching is weak, the card stays provisional instead of pretending the link is settled.

After the meeting

Write back intentionallyPromote only accepted updates into source living docs; keep meeting-local observations separate until reviewed.
Preserve the bridgeThe post-meeting artifact should show which prior docs shaped the frame and which cards should update them.
Refresh the libraryThe meeting doc itself becomes a future context source once its status and access policy are clear.
Failure mode

Ungrounded meeting intelligence

Correct local summaries can still miss why the conversation matters, because the system does not know the prior decision, open proof gap, or unresolved tension.

Value moment

"This changes the existing doc"

The system can surface when a live statement should update a current living doc instead of becoming another isolated meeting note.

Registry role

The registry gives the model safe shapes

Known convergence types tell the runtime where to place context: decision record, finding, proof ladder, theme, stance, citation, or argument frame.

Next step

From static analysis to live meeting collaboration.

The dynamic version would create and revise the living doc during the meeting. It would not replace the conversation. It would give participants a visible analytical surface that can be corrected by voice while there is still time to clarify the claim, challenge an assumption, or ask what evidence would settle it.

1

Live transcript stream enters the doc

Speaker turns arrive with timestamps, confidence, and provisional segmentation.

2

The model proposes cards continuously

Claims, assumptions, and counterarguments appear as draft cards with source spans attached.

3

The meeting can talk back to the frame

Participants can say: that is not our claim, mark that as an assumption, capture the counterargument, or promote this to a decision.

4

The living doc becomes the post-meeting artifact

After the meeting, the team has an auditable reasoning map instead of only a recording and a summary.

Voice collaboration loop

The living doc becomes a second conversational surface.

The meeting still happens in voice, but the structure becomes visible. The group can steer the document in natural language as soon as the model proposes a frame.

"Correct that"Replace a draft claim with the speaker's intended claim while preserving the original transcript span.
"Mark assumption"Promote an unstated premise into the assumptions field instead of letting it remain hidden.
"Add counter"Attach the opposing view to the same claim card, not as a separate note that gets lost.
"Needs evidence"Move a factual assertion into findings or a proof ladder before anyone treats it as settled.
"Decision"When the group agrees, create or update a decision record linked back to the argument that carried it.
Observe

The system listens for structure, not just words

It detects claim candidates, repeated themes, factual assertions, disagreements, and unresolved questions as provisional cards.

Expose

The draft frame appears where everyone can see it

The group sees what the model thinks is happening: claim, assumption, evidence, flaw, counterargument, decision candidate.

Negotiate

Participants correct the frame by voice

Corrections become first-class edits with provenance, not interruptions outside the artifact.

Stabilize

The doc separates settled structure from open structure

Accepted claims, contested claims, open assumptions, and evidence needs stay distinct after the meeting ends.

For facilitatorsSee where the discussion is making claims without evidence, where disagreement is real, and where the group needs a clarifying question.
For participantsInterrupt the structure, not each other: "that is not what I meant" can become an edit instead of a derailment.
For absent readersReview the reasoning path, not just the minutes. The final artifact shows how the meeting arrived at a decision or failed to.
Before the meeting endsOpen assumptions are visible while the right people are still in the room.
After the meetingThe doc separates decisions, claims, evidence needs, and unresolved disagreements.
