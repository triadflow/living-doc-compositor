Tracking a live debate

A proposed living-doc structure for watching the AI-and-labor debate evolve every six months — built on the current synthesis of McAfee, Autor, Acemoglu, Brynjolfsson, and Aghion, and structured to make each check-in a low-friction delta.

Design proposal · structure only

The job

The source economists-ai-labor.html is a one-shot synthesis. It captures five thinkers' stances as of April 2026 and lists the numbers each one points to. Excellent as a snapshot; brittle as a tracker. Every six months the positions shift, papers land, predictions resolve, and a fresh reader has no way to see what changed without re-doing the archaeology.

The job of the living-doc version is different: be the place where the current state of the debate lives, and where each 6-month check-in lands as an inspectable delta. A reader in 2028 should see at a glance who shifted, which predictions resolved, and what's still open — without re-reading three years of source material.

Why this doesn't fit the usual living-doc templates

Most existing templates are bounded by a piece of work that moves toward an outcome: ship the feature, prove the claim, finish the chapter. This doc is bounded by a topic that never ends — the debate keeps going as long as the world keeps producing evidence. The structural consequence is that periods become first-class, alongside sections and facets. Every update must be attributable to a named check-in; every card must be re-derivable from "what did the current period contribute."

The distinctive move. Add periods as a top-level field next to objectiveFacets. Each period has a name (2026-H1), a window (2026-01-01 → 2026-06-30), and a summary. Every card carries lastUpdatedInPeriod so the reader can see what was touched when. Staleness becomes cadence-aware: the doc is "stale" when the next period is due, not when the wall clock hits N days of inactivity.

Objective + facets

Objective. Track the evolution of expert consensus on AI's labor-market impact as evidence accumulates and positions shift — so a reader at any moment knows current stance, latest evidence, and what's still open.

Objective facets

  • current-positions-accurate — each expert's stance reflects their most recent published work
  • predictions-tracked — every "what to watch" claim has a live resolution status
  • indicators-current — the numerical dashboard matches the latest prints
  • divergence-map-current — the agreement/split axes reflect current positions, not stale ones
  • period-attribution — every update traces back to a named monitoring period

Walk-through

1 · Expert positions expert-stance-track

One card per thinker. The existing maintainer-stance type handles one-off positions on a specific thread; this stretches too far. A public intellectual's stance persists across dozens of works and shifts in small steps — we need fields for evolution since last period and a shift flag, not just a current position.

2 · Empirical predictions proof-ladder

The "What to watch in 2026–2027" list is already a prediction tracker in disguise. Each bullet becomes a rung. Rungs accumulate evidence for/against across periods. Resolution status drives the visible state.

claim expectedDirection evidenceRungs[] resolution resolvedInPeriod

Resolution vocabulary: pending · confirmed · refuted · revised. Refuted and revised carry a note explaining why.

3 · Indicator dashboard indicator-trace

Tracked metrics with a dated latest observation and a delta versus the prior period. Existing status sets are categorical (not-started / drafted / final); indicators want numeric status. Two honest ways forward: (a) add a new kind of numeric status field to the registry, or (b) carry the numeric value as a text field and use a categorical trend slot (rising / stable / falling / resolved). Option (b) is cheaper for a first version.

indicator latestValue asOf forecast deltaVsLastPeriod trend source

Candidate indicators: US TFP growth · entry-level employment for 22–25yo in AI-exposed white-collar · wage dispersion within AI-exposed occupations · flexicurity-style wage-insurance adoption · call-centre productivity replications.

4 · Consensus & divergence map position-cluster-map

Two-axis plot. The existing coherence-map does facet-to-section carrying; this needs thinker-to-axis placement, which is a different edge pattern. Could adapt or introduce a small new type. Movement between periods is the whole point — a rendered card should show the prior-period position as a ghost.

axisX axisY thinkers[] positions[{thinker,x,y,period}]

X-axis: magnitude of AI impact (weak → strong). Y-axis: stance on institutional role (market-led → institution-led).

5 · Sources this period citation-feed

Dated source entries grouped by period. The existing source-material patterns support the reference shape; what's new is period-grouping as a first-class organizing principle. Sources added in 2026-H2 live in that period's group; adding a 2026-H1 source retroactively is allowed but flagged.

title author venue publishedAt url cardsReferenced[] addedInPeriod
6 · Period note decision-record

One card per period. Reusing decision-record works because the note is a decision about what the period means. Field fit: the rationale is the shift summary, the context is the evidence that landed, the consequences are what to watch next. Lightly adapted, cleanly reused.

period summary shiftsObserved[] predictionsResolved[] focusForNextPeriod

Periods as first-class

Every card carries a lastUpdatedInPeriod marker. The doc's periods list at the root records each check-in window and its summary.

Renderer implications: "changed this period" should be visually prominent, "unchanged since baseline" should be quiet. The period dropdown at the top lets a reader rewind the whole doc to any prior period for a clean comparison.

Invariants

  • Every expert-position card must cite at least one source dated in the current or prior period — no unchanged snapshots without evidence.
  • Every prediction rung must carry a resolution status.
  • Every indicator card must have a dated latest value.
  • Every source must have a publication date.
  • The period note for the current period must exist once the period closes.
  • The positions[] on the divergence map must include entries for every thinker in this period.

The sync skill

A domain skill — working title /ai-labor-sync — runs on cadence. Given the doc and a period-start date, it performs the archaeology once so the human doesn't have to:

1 · scan
per-thinker feeds
Substacks, NBER, Project Syndicate, major interview outlets, Brookings, PIIE — filtered to the period window.
2 · propose
stance updates
Flags shift candidates per thinker; drafts evolution-since-last-period prose for each.
3 · ingest
new sources
Adds entries to the citation feed, grouped in the current period.
4 · resolve
prediction rungs
Proposes new evidence rungs and resolution transitions; never flips resolution silently.
5 · draft
period note
One-card summary of shifts, resolutions, and focus for next period.

Output is a proposed patch. The human opens the compositor to review, refine, and accept. The skill never mutates the doc directly — it offers a draft the human ratifies.

Honest flags

Numeric status is the real wrinkle. The compositor's status model is categorical. Indicator dashboards naturally want numbers and deltas. The proposal here (carry the number as text, carry direction as a categorical trend) is a workaround, not a clean fit. If longitudinal-monitoring docs become a pattern (this one plus, say, a market-data tracker and a research-claim watcher), the registry probably needs a proper numeric status field.
Renderer wants a period selector. A doc with periods needs a top-level control to rewind the whole view to a prior period — otherwise the "what changed" story is illegible. That's new renderer work, not a registry-only change.
Scope creep risk. This pattern is powerful enough that it'll want to swallow every "watch this over time" use case. Semantic stability wins here too — keep the new types crisp (expert-stance-track, indicator-trace, citation-feed) rather than bundling them into one mega-type that stretches.

One rendered period, imagined

A reader opens the doc in October 2026. At the top, a period selector shows 2026-H2 · current. Three cards carry a "shift" badge — Acemoglu softened, Brynjolfsson held firm, McAfee added nuance on weak-link economics. The predictions ladder shows one rung resolved (the 2025 productivity print persisted into 2026), three pending. The indicator dashboard shows entry-level 22–25yo employment in AI-exposed roles down another ~4%. The divergence map shows Acemoglu moving fractionally toward Aghion on the magnitude axis. The period note says "the productivity-jump-persists prediction is holding; expertise-devaluation evidence is thickening; watch wage dispersion in 2027."

That's what the structure is for. Not to encode the current state prettier than an HTML synthesis page already does, but to make the next check-in a low-friction delta, and the one after that, and the one after that, until the debate either resolves or runs out of steam.
