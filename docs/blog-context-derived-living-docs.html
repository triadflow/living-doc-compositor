Building a living doc from context, before anyone adopts the system.

A living doc is not only for teams that already believe in living docs. It can also be built from conversations, artifacts, tickets, and surrounding evidence as an explicit model of someone's method, system, or working reality.

RL
Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedApr 23, 2026 Read10 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-context-derived-living-docs.html

A lot of useful systems exist before their authors ever formalize them. A founder has a method. A team has a rhythm. A builder has a theory of control. An operator has a way of deciding what counts as evidence. You can often see all of this in conversation, in tickets, in repos, in strategy notes, in issue threads, in live artifacts. The person may not have built a living doc. That does not mean you have to wait for one before the system becomes legible.

This is a use case I think matters more than it first sounds. A living doc is usually described as a coordination surface for insiders: the team adopts the format, keeps it current, and works from it. That is the obvious mode. But there is another one. You can build a living doc from context as an external model of a person, a project, a team, or a method. Not as fiction. Not as branding. As a disciplined working model of what the available evidence says is going on.

The crucial point is that the result should still be a normal living doc. It should say what the work is trying to make true, what appears to be true now, what evidence supports that reading, what is unresolved, and what next move would create value. The outsider-specific layers belong in the preparation around the doc, not as durable first-class structure inside it.

The use case starts before adoption

Very often the moment you most need a living doc is the moment before the subject would ever agree to maintain one. You are trying to assess a collaborator. You are trying to understand a founder's method. You are trying to evaluate whether a team's architecture is ahead of its proof surface. You are trying to prepare for a serious conversation with someone whose work already spans chats, repos, captures, notes, decisions, and contradictory explanations.

In those situations, “wait until they adopt the system” is not a useful answer. You need a coherent model now. You need to know what they are trying to do, what structure they think the work has, where the constraints are, what they believe counts as evidence, and where reality appears to differ from ambition. A context-derived living doc can do that.

The key shift

A living doc is not only a coordination surface for insiders. It is also a modeling surface for outsiders. The same format that helps a team run the work can help an observer understand the work before the team has formalized it themselves.

What kind of document this is

A context-derived living doc is not the same thing as a self-maintained living doc. That distinction matters. A self-maintained doc can usually speak in a more direct voice because the author is asserting the structure from inside the system. But the resulting artifact should still converge on the same kind of object: a living doc about the work itself.

What changes is the preparation around that object. The observer needs a separate derivation note or working memo that keeps track of provenance. Which parts came from direct statements? Which parts came from visible artifacts? Which parts are our structural inference? Which parts remain unknown? A good context-derived pass is stronger not because it pushes those layers into the doc, but because it keeps them explicit beside the doc.

Preparation layerWhat belongs thereWhy it stays outside the living doc
ObservedDirect statements, tickets, code, artifacts, cited notes, visible statusKeeps the work doc from turning interpretation into fact
InferredLikely objective, control model, value model, architectural readingKeeps the doc from pretending the subject already agrees with our framing
UnknownMissing metrics, hidden decisions, unverified live state, absent artifactsKeeps the doc from claiming false completeness
ContestedSignals that conflict or imply different readingsKeeps contradiction from being smoothed into the work surface too early

Why this is useful

The value is not “better notes from a conversation.” The value is that the living doc forces the interpretation to take shape. If you build a document around someone's method, you have to answer hard questions the conversation may never answer directly. What are the units of work? What is the control surface? What is the proof model? What are the real open decisions? Which parts are already operating, and which parts are only planned? Where is the system strongest? Where is it still aspirational?

Those questions are exactly the questions you usually need in order to assess whether an idea is serious, whether a method is coherent, whether a partnership is worth entering, or whether someone's operating model is likely to create value. The living doc becomes a disciplined synthesis, not a transcript with bullet points.

What the document is really modeling

A context-derived living doc is often best when it does not try to model everything. The strongest versions usually model one of four things.

  • A method. How does this person think work should move? Proof-first, system-first, output-first, claim-first, governance-first.
  • A system. What are the major moving parts, how do they relate, and where is the current maturity uneven?
  • A decision field. What choices are still holding the architecture open, and what evidence would close them?
  • A value loop. What small repeatable actions create useful output, and what larger machine is being built around them?

Often the most revealing thing is not the subject's stated objective but the shape of the document that emerges when you try to model them. If the doc naturally fills with teams, pipelines, and tools, you are probably looking at a machine-building mindset. If it naturally fills with outputs, proof loops, and outcome gates, you are probably looking at an output-first method. The modeling process itself becomes diagnostic, which is exactly the point of what living docs reveal.

The danger: projection dressed up as structure

This use case has a real danger. The more disciplined the document looks, the easier it becomes to mistake your own interpretation for the subject's actual system. A well-structured proxy can become persuasive enough that even the author forgets where the structure came from.

That is why epistemic honesty is not an optional nicety here. It is the whole thing. If the document is not explicit about where the readings come from, the format becomes a projection machine. You can make anyone look coherent if you are willing to erase uncertainty. You can also make anyone look worse than they are by turning provisional impressions into rigid categories.

The antidote is not vagueness. The antidote is provenance and corrigibility. A good context-derived living doc should be sharp enough to say, this is our best current model, while also making it easy to challenge that model.

A good proxy doc stays corrigible

The best sign that a context-derived living doc is healthy is that it invites correction without collapsing. It can say, “this objective is inferred from the conversation and the artifacts we have,” and if the subject disagrees, the disagreement improves the doc rather than invalidating the whole exercise.

What sources are enough to begin

You do not need omniscience to start. Usually you need a coherent enough source envelope that a real structure begins to repeat. That might include conversations, issue threads, architecture notes, repos, screenshots, public posts, decision captures, or work artifacts. The important thing is not the prestige of the source. It is whether the source bears on the shape of the system you are trying to model.

What usually matters most is a mix of surfaces. Conversation alone is too aspirational. Repos alone are too implementation-biased. Tickets alone can overstate open questions and understate operating rhythm. The document gets sharper when multiple surfaces converge on the same reading.

Why the living doc format is unusually good for this

A lot of formats can collect evidence. Fewer formats are good at making the evidence hold a coherent model. The reason the living doc format works well here is that it forces the observer to choose a bounded scope, choose explicit section types, choose statuses, and choose what counts as unresolved instead of leaving everything in formless notes.

That boundedness matters. The doc does not have to answer every question about the person or team. It only has to answer the question this modeled scope is for. That is why the result can be dense without collapsing into biography. You are not trying to explain the whole subject. You are trying to make one method, one system, one workstream, or one decision field legible enough to inspect.

How AI helps build the first model

This is one of the places where AI is genuinely helpful. A pass like /context-derived-doc can read across the allowed source set much faster than a person wants to, propose the first normal living doc, and produce a separate derivation note that tracks observed material, inferred structure, unknowns, and contested readings. That is valuable because the first hard step is usually not insight. It is getting enough of the structure onto the page that real correction becomes possible without letting the work doc turn into a meta artifact.

The human role does not disappear. It becomes sharper. The human sets the boundary, decides what sources are legitimate, rejects false structure, and corrects the draft where the inference overreaches. The living doc is the bounded surface where broad AI reading meets human judgment and becomes something both can improve.

We ran into the same need inside this series. While drafting these essays and the related skills, the interpretation layer started outrunning the work. The moment we built a living doc for the Reading Systems effort itself, the objective, current state, boundary decisions, and next checks came back into view. That is one reason I trust this mode. When the model starts floating away from the work, the living doc exposes it.

What happens next if the subject engages

One of the strongest things about this mode is that it does not become useless the moment the subject enters the conversation. Quite the opposite. The document becomes a correction surface. The person can say, “that objective is wrong,” “that pipeline is not real,” “this section is too architecture-heavy,” “that status should be partial, not operational,” or “those two decisions are the same decision.” Every correction makes the model more valuable.

That means the lifecycle can go in phases, often ending in the kind of serious conversation described in Come prepared to the table:

  1. Build a living doc from context as an outsider's working model.
  2. Use it to assess the method, system, or value model.
  3. Bring it into conversation with the subject if useful.
  4. Let the document either stay an analyst's surface or transition into a shared one.

The important point is that usefulness starts before shared ownership does. The doc does not need unanimous buy-in before it starts creating clarity.

The document becomes useful at the moment it is explicit enough to be wrong in public.

— because correction is part of the method, not a failure of it

What this reveals that conversation alone usually doesn't

People can speak fluently about their systems without ever making the deep structure visible. They can talk about strategy, workflow, principles, autonomy, quality, or shipping. The living doc forces those words to cash out into explicit units. What are the moving parts? What is central? What is evidence? What is blocked? What is merely planned? What is the actual sequence by which value is expected to appear?

Once the document exists, you can often see mismatches that were blurry in conversation. A person may talk output-first while the model that emerges is architecture-first. A team may talk evidence while the only visible proof surface is notes and belief. A method may sound coherent until you try to place the open decisions and discover that the control model is still unresolved. The format does not create these tensions; it exposes them.

The deeper claim

I think the deeper claim is simple. A living doc is not only a tool for people who have already adopted the living-doc way of working. It is also a tool for seeing systems that have not yet made themselves explicit. That matters because many of the most important judgments happen before adoption: whether to collaborate, whether to invest, whether to challenge a method, whether a machine is creating value or only promising it.

If the doc is built honestly, with clear provenance and explicit inference, it becomes a serious analytical surface. It lets you assess a person's method without pretending to inhabit their head. It lets you model a team's system without pretending the system is settled. It lets you discover where the structure is real and where it is still wishful. And if the subject later joins the process, the same document can become the place where the model gets corrected into something stronger.

The practical standard

A context-derived living doc should be strong enough to reveal the method and humble enough to reveal the uncertainty. If it cannot do both, it is either too weak to matter or too confident to trust.
