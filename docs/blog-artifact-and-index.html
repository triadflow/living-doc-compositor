The artifact and the index: composing living docs with a knowledge graph.

Portable living docs and queryable knowledge graphs look like competing architectures. They are two halves of the same quadrant — and the split tells you what each one is for.

RL
Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedApr 19, 2026 Read10 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-artifact-and-index.html

There is a conversation that keeps happening in different rooms. In one room, someone describes a portable living document — a single file you can email, open offline, or load into any agent — and someone else asks: why wouldn't you just put all of that in a knowledge graph? In another room, someone describes an enterprise knowledge graph with context graphs sitting alongside operational databases and data warehouses, and someone else asks: why wouldn't you just write a readable document that already contains what the reader needs? The two rooms sound like they disagree. They don't. They're looking at two different halves of the same picture, and naming the split is the interesting part.

This post sits that conversation down. It uses a framework that's been going around thanks to a recent Emil Eifrem interview — the four quadrants of agent data sources — and it uses one person's running stack to show that the two architectures compose cleanly once you stop mistaking them for competitors.

The four quadrants, briefly

Emil's framing goes like this. As soon as you try to deploy an agent that does real work in a real organization, it needs access to four kinds of data, and each one has a different shape.

QuadrantWhat it holdsExample question
Operational database (OLTP) System of record for the present How many customers do I have right now?
Data warehouse (OLAP) System of record for the past How much revenue did we earn in LatAm last quarter?
Agentic memory Agent state, short- and long-term What did this agent learn about me last week?
Context graph Institutional knowledge, decision traces, the why Why did we ship at 20% off list when policy says 10%?

The sharp one is the fourth. Emil's example: a sales rep closes a deal at a non-standard discount because the VP approved it over Slack. Salesforce records the what. It does not record the why. As decision-making shifts from human brains to agent brains, that missing why becomes the bottleneck.

Living docs are a realization of this fourth quadrant — crystallized decisions about sources, convergence types, invariants, coverage. That framing is accurate but past-tense. It undersells what the format is doing while the work is still happening, which is where the purpose lives.

What a living doc is for

A living doc exists to solve a specific problem — and to be the state keeper for that problem while a human and one or more AI agents work on it together. That's the core purpose. Everything else in this post (portability, convergence types, graph indexing) is architecture in service of that purpose.

The concrete workflow looks like this. I have a piece of work: a stuck bug, a research claim to defend, a chapter to finish, a pipeline to harden. I open a living doc for it. I also open Claude Code in one terminal and Codex in another. All three of us — me, Claude Code, Codex — read the same doc. None of us is the single source of truth. The doc is. And as the work progresses, the doc moves: status transitions land, attempts get logged, decisions get recorded, coverage gets drawn, invariants get flagged when violated. The updates come from whichever of the three of us is doing the move in that moment. The other two see the new state on the next read.

That's what makes it living. Not the file format. Not the convergence types. The fact that the doc is being kept current, continuously, by the actual team working on the problem — where the team is a human plus the agents they've invited in.

Three jobs, one artifact

Solve the problem. The doc is attached to a specific outcome. The convergence types aren't decoration; they specify what shape of progress counts for this kind of work.

Keep state while doing it. The doc updates during the work, not at the end. Every session can land edits. Staleness is a signal that the work has moved and the doc hasn't caught up.

Coordinate the human-agent team. One file, shared scope, typed cards. When Claude Code asks "where am I," the doc answers. When I ask "what did Codex do in the last session," the doc shows it. When the two agents run in parallel on adjacent parts of the problem, the doc is what stops them from stepping on each other.

Once that purpose is named, the architectural properties stop being abstract. Portable, because the team moves between environments. Typed, because the team includes agents that need machine-readable structure. Bounded, because a working surface needs to be small enough for a human plus two agents to hold in mind in one session. Each is a requirement the purpose imposes, not an aesthetic choice. The scope disagreement in the next section is not about database patterns; it's about what kind of surface a human-agent team needs to get work done.

Where the structure comes from

There's a step I skipped when I said "I open a living doc for it." I want to walk that back. Living docs don't start from a blank canvas — at least, not the ones that work. The initial structure is usually discovered by an AI skill before a human touches it, and then refined in the compositor if the discovery missed something.

This matters because it answers a question Emil flagged as the biggest unsolved problem with enterprise context graphs: how do you even start the record of decision traces? His honest answer was "that ends up being the interesting discussion" — in other words, nobody has a general solution. Our answer works because we shrank the scope. At the scope of one piece of work, an AI skill can read enough source material in a single pass to propose a real typed structure. Bootstrapping gets tractable when you stop trying to bootstrap everything at once.

A few concrete examples:

  • A deep-dive skill reads the problem. /oss-issue-deep-dive reads a GitHub issue, its comments, the linked PRs, the referenced code, the sibling issues, and composes a doc with typed sections — symptoms, findings, code anchors, attempts, maintainer stances, orbit issues. The Textual #5380 doc I've referenced throughout this post was born that way. The human didn't pick which sections to have; the skill proposed them based on what the archaeology turned up.
  • A crystallize skill names the governance. /crystallize runs on an existing doc that has stabilized and proposes the governance layer: objective facets derived from the doc's objective statement, coverage edges mapping facets to the sections that carry them, invariants the doc already follows implicitly, rationale for each section's convergence-type choice. The doc gets a constitution without me having to write one.
  • Domain skills do their own version. Pipelines, face recognition, market-data research, chapter drafting — each has or can have a skill that scans its source material and proposes the right convergence types. The skill is domain-specific; the target format is shared.

The compositor is the refinement surface. Once a skill has proposed a structure, I open the doc there to review and correct — drag a card between sections, change a convergence type, add an invariant, fix a coverage edge. The skill drafts like an intern; the compositor is where the senior editor makes it right.

The lifecycle, end to end

(1) A skill scans the problem and proposes a typed doc. (2) The human reviews and refines in the compositor if needed. (3) The doc becomes the working surface for the problem, kept current by the human-agent team. (4) A sync layer indexes the doc into whatever graph the portfolio runs on. (5) When the work ends, the doc stays as the record of choices made.

Each step involves AI. Each step leaves a typed artifact. No step requires the human to build from scratch.

That division of labor matters for the architectural argument coming next. A doc proposed by a skill and refined in the compositor is cheap; that cheapness is what makes the "many small projections" scaling story below work. If spinning up a fresh bounded scope weren't cheap, the team wouldn't bother, and the whole argument collapses.

One big graph, or many named bounded ones

Here is where the two architectures part ways.

The knowledge graph, as typically pitched by graph-database vendors, wants to be the coherent enterprise index. One graph, billions of nodes, cross-domain entity resolution, a business-facing ontology hammered into place over months of stakeholder negotiation. What is a customer. How does a customer relate to a supplier. Five people give six answers. The graph forces convergence, and the convergence is the product. Scale grows by growing the graph.

Living docs go the other way. Each doc is a named, bounded projection of a piece of work. It has its own scope: this feature, that issue, this chapter, that research claim. Its sources are explicit and limited. Its convergence types come from a shared registry — a small, governed vocabulary — but the doc itself is a subgraph, not a slice of one enormous one. You don't need the whole enterprise ontology to open it. You need one file. Scale grows by multiplying projections, not by growing a single graph.

That difference looks small until you walk through the consequences.

  • The enterprise graph must be queried — it's too big to read. The living doc must be read — it fits on a page.
  • The enterprise graph answers cross-domain questions by walking relationships. The living doc answers bounded questions by already containing the current state.

Neither is better. They are different levers. The mistake is using either one for the job the other is shaped for.

The enterprise graph scales by growing. The living doc scales by multiplying.

— Two different axes, not two sides of the same axis.

Showing the composition: one person's running stack

Here is where I have to be careful about scope. Everything in this section is outside living-doc-compositor. The compositor ships one thing: the typed, portable artifact. What you index it against — what graph you feed the artifacts into, if any — is an architectural choice you make. The rest of this post describes my own choice, running today, as evidence that the pattern composes. It is not a compositor feature. Readers who adopt the compositor do not inherit this infrastructure.

Here's the setup. Alongside the living docs, I run a Project Graph — a Neo4j database that indexes tickets, commits, PRs, sessions, concepts, decisions, and documents across all my repos. It answers cross-artifact questions: which tickets touch this workstream, which sessions contributed to this doc, which concepts are co-mentioned, which decisions still hold. No single living doc can answer those; they span projections.

Between the two sits a sync layer — in my case an MCP server called living-docs-mcp that reads typed blocks out of each doc and syncs them bidirectionally with WorkCluster nodes in the graph. A launchd agent runs a health check on that sync, so drift surfaces immediately.

None of that plumbing matters. The pattern matters:

  1. The compositor produces typed, portable artifacts. This is the authoring surface.
  2. A sync layer — whatever you write, in whatever language, against whatever graph — reads the typed structure out of each artifact and indexes it.
  3. The graph becomes a union index of everything the artifacts declared. This is the navigation surface.

You can realize step two against Neo4j, Dgraph, SQLite with a graph schema, a Postgres table with foreign keys, or a file of JSON-LD. The compositor doesn't care. The typed registry is what makes the sync tractable — a convergence type is a contract, a status set is a vocabulary, a coverage edge is an explicit relationship. The sync reads the contract and writes it into whatever substrate you prefer.

Zero-copy, at the artifact layer

Emil borrows a term Salesforce popularized: zero-copy. The knowledge layer doesn't replicate the data; it points at the source. Our composition does the same thing one layer up. The graph indexes the artifact, not the content. The artifact stays the source of truth; the graph stays a navigation layer. If the artifact is updated, the graph re-indexes. The two never disagree for long, because only one of them can be edited.

What each surface does that the other can't

Once the composition is in place, it becomes obvious which surface is shaped for which job.

JobArtifactGraph
Load as working context for a session Yes — one file, one scope, human-readable No — too big, needs a query to land
Travel offline, by email, in Slack Yes — self-contained HTML No — requires the database
Check coherence within its own scope Yes — fingerprint, invariants, coverage Awkward — coherence lives per projection, not per graph
Answer "which tickets touch which living docs" No — no doc sees the others Yes — one query across the union
Find where a concept is mentioned across all work No Yes — concept nodes with edges to every mention
Preserve decisions and stance for future readers Yes — typed cards with rationale Indirect — the graph mirrors what the artifact declared
Navigate: "what am I working on, what's stale, where's my gap" No — each doc is local Yes — cross-artifact dashboard

Read that table and the conversation those two rooms were having falls apart. The first room's question — why wouldn't you just put it in a graph? — assumes the job is navigation. For that job, a graph is right. The second room's question — why wouldn't you just write a document? — assumes the job is loading context for a bounded piece of work. For that job, a document is right. Neither room was wrong; they were describing different columns of the table.

The pattern crosses domains

This is the part that tends to surprise people who come at living docs from one specific domain.

My own living-docs index, which the sync layer reads from, currently spans: a market-data research substrate, a geometric-model evaluation framework, a face-recognition pipeline, a code-review workstream, an identity-hardening initiative, a book-writing project with chapter-level docs, a music-brand recognition pipeline, a parser's proof ladder, and a handful more. The docs are not all about software. The substrate is not all code.

What makes the composition work across those domains is that the convergence types are shared. A proof ladder is the same shape whether it's the rungs of evidence behind a parser claim or the rungs of evidence behind a research finding. A decision record is the same shape whether it's an architecture call or an editorial call on a chapter. A code anchor is just an entity-reference under a different name; swap code anchor for scene anchor and the card's structural contract still holds.

This is the point Emil's enterprise-knowledge-graph pitch can't reach. His pitch needs the ontology to be specific — "customer," "supplier," "account" — because the graph is bound to one organization's idea of what those things mean. The living-doc registry runs the other way. The convergence types are deliberately abstract. Proof ladder. Capability surface. Decision record. Attempt log. Issue orbit. Content outline. They name a shape of work, not a domain object. That's why the same registry can carry pipeline engineering, manuscript architecture, and market-data research without conflict.

When the sync layer walks those docs and writes them into the graph, the graph inherits the shared vocabulary. I can ask "which proof ladders are currently unfinished across all my work" and get back rungs from a parser project, a trading-model research effort, and a chapter claim. The enterprise ontology cannot answer that question because it has no shared vocabulary across those domains. The living-doc registry does.

The registry names shapes of work. That is what lets the same vocabulary carry engineering, research, writing, and operations without collapsing them into one another.

What this implies for agents

The human-agent team from earlier — me plus Claude Code plus Codex — needs both halves, for different moments of the work. When one of us opens a session, we load the living doc; the three of us read the same file and start in "where does the fix go" mode rather than "what is this project" mode. That's the state-keeper job while the work is active.

When I need to reason across work — what's stale, what's blocked, which concept is showing up everywhere, which doc I should even be opening — I query the graph. It tells me which artifact to open; we load it and work inside it. The graph is the map; the surface is where the session happens. Two interaction modes, two substrates, one shared vocabulary — the registry — that makes them cohere.

The failure mode I keep seeing in 2026 is teams picking one side of the table and complaining about the other. Lean only on the enterprise graph and your agents drown in traversals, because the navigation layer can't carry a session's worth of bounded reasoning. Lean only on self-contained docs and your agents answer bounded questions beautifully but have no idea which one to work on. The halves aren't interchangeable. The fix isn't to pick one; it's to compose, and to let the living doc be where the human plus Claude Code plus Codex keep state.

What this doesn't solve, and what it asks for

Three honest limits. Entity resolution across projections. As the number of docs grows, the same real-world thing shows up in several under slightly different names. The compositor doesn't solve this; the sync layer can, if you write it with resolution in mind. Living docs rot without editors. The "living" part is a social commitment, not a file-format feature. Agents can flag staleness and propose status updates, but something has to trigger the review — freshness queries across the graph are exactly what the index is for. The sync layer is real engineering. A minimal version is a few hundred lines against your chosen substrate; a serious one is months. The post's claim isn't that you get this for free — it's that the abstraction is sound enough to be worth the build.

The deeper shift

The two architectures people keep arguing about aren't opposed. They're halves of the same quadrant. The portable artifact holds institutional knowledge at the scope of a single piece of work. The queryable index holds institutional knowledge at the scope of a whole portfolio. Compose them and you get both, without picking the wrong tool for the other job.

The registry is what makes the composition cheap. A small, shared vocabulary of work shapes is the thing that carries across domains and into whatever index you run. The rest is plumbing you can write against anything.

Where this leaves the conversation

If you're building for the context-graph quadrant — decision traces, institutional memory, the why behind work — you don't have to choose between a graph that's hard to load and a doc that's hard to query across. You can have the doc as the authoring surface, the graph as the navigation surface, and one registry of shared work shapes holding them together. The compositor gives you the first half; the second is yours to build, against the index you trust. Neither needs the other to exist — but put them together and neither is asked to do the other's job. That's when the quadrant starts paying off.
