Strategy deck · 2026-04-20

Grace Andrews's playbook — applied to the Living Doc Compositor

Ten principles from a 1h 21min interview with the brand director who grew Diary of a CEO from 8K to 13M. Each one translated into what it could mean for a typed-ontology OSS tool aimed at cross-domain complex-work. Not a to-do list — a map of bets worth considering.

01

Growth = consistency × experimentation

"If you just keep being consistent, you will just plateau. The actual formula for growth is consistency × experimentation."

What this means for the compositor

We already post consistently. But not every post names the bet. "Registry is a language" and "Artifact & index" both landed, but we can't say which ingredient did the work. Each post should declare what it's testing: a new convergence-type example, a new reader-entry format, a new medium (dossier vs long-form vs screenshot-driven).

Bet to consider Add a Hypothesis field to the editorial living doc: every pipeline card states what's being tested and what signal would validate it. Review monthly against the registry of bets.
02

Measure the metric your goal needs — not what the platform celebrates

"Instagram telling me it had been a success based on followers. Against the metrics I laid out, it still didn't perform. We've got to know what you're measuring."

What this means for the compositor

Page views on a blog post are our "Instagram followers." If the goal is people adopting the compositor to organise their own complex work, the metrics are different: number of external JSON docs rendered, GitHub forks, template downloads, unsolicited "I'm using this for X" signals, inbound DMs.

One metric per post. Declared in advance. Measured after.

Bet to consider Top of each blog post's editorial card: the metric this post is optimising for. If a post about monitoring trackers is trying to produce one serious fork adopting the monitoring type, page views don't count.
03

Memorable minutes beat forgettable seconds

"Virality is a sugar rush. If you've just spent an hour with me, we've gone from zero to trust. Through short form, that's going to take weeks, months, years."

What this means for the compositor

This is actually our structural advantage. A rendered living doc is a one-asset hour: sidebar nav, compositor overlay, pencil editing, embedded templates. If a reader opens mcp-protocol-monitor.html and spends 20 minutes poking around, that's a memorable-minutes conversion no blog thread ever gets.

Most of our content is short-form about the tool. The tool itself is the long-form. We should shift gravity toward: every post ends by putting the reader inside a living doc, not just on a landing page.

Bet to consider Instrument depth-of-engagement on rendered docs (scroll depth, section clicks, compositor-overlay open) — not page views. That's the honest signal. File file:// won't report back; hosted versions will.
04

The "almost game" — volume negates luck

"If we message a thousand people, we're going to get 10 people who say yes. We'd message a thousand people every single week."

What this means for the compositor

We ship posts with care, but our outbound is zero. The compositor has no dedicated DM program to people who'd plausibly use it. Grace's team hit Steve's DM limit and made new accounts. Our equivalent: reaching out to researchers, authors, operations teams, OSS maintainers, therapists, policy analysts — anyone running complex work across sources.

Three reach-outs a week gets us approximately nowhere. Twenty a week for a month would be informative regardless of who says yes.

Bet to consider Build a prospect living doc (convergence type: contact feed?) with 200 named candidates who'd benefit from the system. Message at volume. Track which archetype converts (researcher vs ops vs writer). Let the data pick the audience, not our intuition.
05

Reach the target by proximity, not cold ask

"We wanted Obama. We went to Boris Johnson first to prove we could have a political conversation. Then the person in the middle goes, oh, I've seen that show."

What this means for the compositor

Who's the "Obama" of our space? The person whose endorsement (or just use) would unlock everyone downstream. Candidates: Simon Willison, Andy Matuschak, Maggie Appleton, Linus Lee, the Ink & Switch lab, Steph Ango (Obsidian). Going straight to them as strangers is low-probability.

Adjacent-credibility chain: who do they already follow who'd plausibly use the compositor first? Ship the tool to that person. Then the target sees it twice-removed and we're not a stranger.

Bet to consider Name the three targets that would unlock a wave. Map their influence graph (5 people each). Seed those 15 first with tailored templates. Track who posts publicly. Second hop reaches the target with credibility attached.
06

Pre-production testing — kill the guesswork

"We were testing 100 thumbnails per episode. Wouldn't it be a shame if this episode never saw the light of day because we didn't get the title right?"

What this means for the compositor

We draft blog titles and hit publish. Grace's team would generate 100 titles and test before publishing. For us this could be: draft 5 headlines + 5 opening paragraphs per post, pick via a quick poll (the existing Session Messenger contacts are a test audience).

Same logic for templates. We build a monitoring-tracker template because it feels like the next one. Pre-test: if we only described the idea as a tweet, which of four candidate types gets the most "I'd use this" replies?

Bet to consider Before the next template build, run a one-screen "which would you use" test against 3–5 candidates. Build the winner first.
07

Chase industry-building, not accolades

"It was never about chasing. It was like growing the industry. That's what fuelled us — you can just keep going for a lot longer."

What this means for the compositor

The project's intrinsic mission is shared semantic vocabulary for complex work across domains — book, research, ops, code. That's already the internal compass (see project_convergence_type_tradeoff). When GitHub stars tick up, the right response is "cool, what's the next type?" — not "we made it."

The tell: when we stop inventing new convergence types and start polishing the existing eight, we've drifted into accolade-chasing.

Bet to consider Keep a running "what domain haven't we typed yet?" list. Legal case work. Clinical notes. Sports coaching. Novel drafting. Each one unexplored = the industry isn't shaped yet.
08

The city/subway map — goal, signal, content

"Grand Central is your why. Destinations are your goals. Lines are your signals. Stops are your content formats. Timetable is your content plan."

What this means for the compositor

Why (Grand Central): make complex work legible to humans and LLMs via typed ontology.

  • Destination A — OSS adoption by knowledge workers (not engineers). Signal: personal brand authority in complex-work circles. Formats: long-form posts with dogfooded examples, dossier shorts.
  • Destination B — content creators using compositor for research. Signal: visible dogfooding by high-taste operators. Formats: Grace-style mini-case-studies of real docs in the wild.
  • Destination C — academic / policy / research orgs. Signal: citable methodology. Formats: a Zenodo-indexed technical paper on the typed-ontology approach.
Bet to consider Draft this as an actual compositor living doc. It dogfoods the method and becomes the project's internal operating artifact. Review monthly, re-plot quarterly.
09

Build a confidence bank

"I have a confidence bank in my notes app — a bank of positive feedback. Cold, hard truth that can't be denied. Collecting that evidence balances the noise."

What this means for the compositor

Every unsolicited "this is how I'm thinking about my book now," every fork from someone unexpected, every DM that says "I used this for X" — capture it. Not for ego. For signal: which archetype converts, which framing lands, which template got shared.

When the next blog post underperforms and the doubt creeps in, the confidence bank is evidence that the direction is right.

Bet to consider A dedicated living doc of convergence type citation-feed: dated entries, each with the quote, the source, and which principle it validates. Links back to the map in §08.
10

Start before you're ready — document the mess

"Entrepreneurship is glamorised. I want to show the week I haven't got dressed in my bad office. That's more important than the shiny success."

What this means for the compositor

We already do the mess documentation internally — the blog-editorial.json living doc with 15 voice invariants and six progressive edit-pass cards is exactly this. The question is whether we put it on the blog or keep it in the drawer.

Posting "Here's how I edited six blog posts against 15 voice invariants, with diffs" is unusually honest content. Most OSS projects don't show their editorial layer.

Bet to consider Publish the editorial living doc itself as a blog piece — not a summary of it, the doc. Readers see the process alongside the output. Dogfood at maximum transparency.

Next step: pick two or three of these to run this quarter, not all ten. Grace's own model is three destinations per season. The cost of running all ten at low intensity is higher than running three seriously.
