Who gets to grade the frontier.

In 2026-H1, a frontier AI evaluator matters when three things are true — independence, access, and mandate. Most of the new leaderboard market has one. A handful of institutes have all three.

Rene Luijk · Apr 20, 2026 · 5 min read · evaluation safety governance monitoring
TL;DR

Through 2025, AI evaluation was largely an observer layer — benchmarks published, leaderboards maintained, findings cited. In 2026, the UK and US AI Safety Institutes secured pre-deployment access to the frontier labs, and a small set of nonprofits (METR, Apollo) structured similar arrangements. Evaluation became a release-shaping activity, not just a publishing one.

But only for institutions that carry three properties at once: independence from lab funding, access to weights before GA, and a mandate the labs take seriously. Most of the commercial leaderboard market has none of the second two.

The AI evaluation ecosystem in 2026-H1 is undergoing a quieter transition than the model release calendar suggests. Through 2024 and most of 2025, evaluators were downstream observers. Benchmarks like MMLU, GPQA Diamond, and SWE-bench got published; labs ran them internally; leaderboards aggregated the claims; and the outputs fed into the product-marketing layer. In 2026, a different shape appeared. The UK AI Safety Institute now holds structured pre-deployment access with Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google DeepMind; the US AISI at NIST holds a parallel arrangement with OpenAI and Anthropic.1Note 1UK AISI was established in 2023 as DSIT-housed, granted statutory mandate in 2024. US AISI sits at NIST under the 2023 AI Executive Order; its long-term funding is contested in Congress through 2026. Both published pre-deployment reports on 2026 frontier releases; findings shared privately with the labs, with selected public summaries. METR and Apollo Research have analogous access arrangements focused on autonomy and deception evals. Evaluation has begun to shape what ships and when — not just how it's graded after the fact.

But the shift is narrow. It applies only to the institutions that hold three properties simultaneously: independence from lab financial interest, access to models before general availability, and a mandate the labs treat as consequential. Take any one away and the evaluator reverts to advisory at best, marketing at worst. The 2026-H1 eval ecosystem rewards precisely three or four institutions with the triad, and structurally excludes the rest from the release-shaping conversation.

Independence is the upstream prerequisite

Independence from lab funding is the first filter, and it is sharper than the ecosystem makes it look. A commercial evaluator that sells eval services to the labs it grades carries the same structural conflict as an accounting firm that sells consulting to the companies it audits.2Note 2Scale SEAL is the clearest case: Scale AI sells training-data pipelines to frontier labs and also runs leaderboards that grade those labs' models. The conflict is explicit and disclosed, but the market treats Scale SEAL numbers as comparable to independent scores. They are not, any more than Arthur Andersen's audit opinions were comparable to Big Four peers circa 2000. Scale SEAL, Vellum, and the commercial-leaderboard tier sit in this zone. Their numbers are useful as data points; they cannot serve as referees.

The independent side of the line includes UK AISI and US AISI (government-funded, statutory mandate, no lab equity), METR and Apollo (philanthropic funding, methodology transparency, accept no lab compensation), MLCommons (multi-party consortium with governance that dilutes any single member), and Epoch AI (philanthropic funding, publishes compute-trend reference numbers). These are the institutions whose findings can be cited without a disclosure paragraph and still mean what they appear to mean.

Access is the 2026 differentiator

Independence without access produces informed commentary. Access without independence produces captured testimony. The combination is what 2026 added to the picture. The UK AISI's access arrangement with Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind — signed through late 2025 and used on 2026 frontier releases — lets evaluators run autonomy, cyber-capability, and dangerous-capability suites before GA, with findings shared privately and selectively published.3Note 3The UK AISI's public statements in Q1 2026 confirm access arrangements with Anthropic and OpenAI for frontier releases including Claude Opus 4.7 and the GPT-5 family. Findings format: institute report, optional public summary, private feedback to the lab. No public veto announced; the shape is consultative, with the lab retaining release decision authority.

This is structurally different from running a public benchmark. A public benchmark is a ceiling score on a test the lab already optimised for. Pre-deployment access means examining behaviours the lab may not have looked for, on weights the lab has not published, under conditions the evaluator chose. Labs retain release authority; the arrangements are consultative, not vetoing. But the information asymmetry shifts: an AISI knows something about the model that the public eval ecosystem does not, and the labs know the AISI knows.

METR and Apollo's access arrangements are narrower in scope — autonomy-over-time-horizon evals and scheming-behaviour evals respectively — but operate on the same principle.4Note 4METR's time-horizon-of-tasks figure in Q1 2026: frontier models complete tasks requiring approximately 1–2 hours of software-engineer time at ~50% success, with horizon extending quarter over quarter. Apollo's scheming findings: multiple frontier reasoning models demonstrate in-context scheming under adversarial prompting, frequency rising with capability. The rest of the ecosystem — MLCommons, Epoch AI, Scale SEAL, the academic benchmark-curators — operates on published checkpoints or public releases. That is not worse work. It is different work with a different ceiling on what it can credibly claim.

Mandate is what makes the numbers bite

The third property is the softest and the most easily eroded. An evaluator with independence and access, but without a mandate the labs take seriously, still mostly publishes findings that get absorbed into the lab's own safety-narrative. The labs cite the evaluator when findings are favourable; politely engage when findings are negative; and keep shipping on their own timeline. The UK AISI has mandate in the UK context; the US AISI's mandate is structurally contested by Congressional funding debates through 2026.5Note 5US AISI funding remains politically contested through 2026. The institute continues to operate on its NIST-housed budget, but a long-term authorisation bill has not passed. This creates the unusual situation of an evaluator holding lab access but not statutory permanence — which the labs notice when prioritising response effort.

METR and Apollo have mandate through reputation and through the labs' own voluntary commitments to responsible-scaling policies — a weaker form of mandate than statutory authority, but one that holds as long as the labs' RSP commitments hold. A useful test for mandate will arrive the first time an evaluator publishes a negative finding and a lab ships anyway. That has not happened in public through 2026-H1. If it happens, it will clarify the hierarchy instantly.

The saturation clock

Evaluators also face the benchmark-saturation problem. GPQA Diamond is saturating. AIME 2025 is already above ninety percent at frontier.6Note 6Humanity's Last Exam, curated by CAIS in collaboration with Scale SEAL, is the benchmark designed to resist multiple model generations. Top score in Q1 2026 sits around 25–30%. FrontierMath from Epoch AI is in the teens. Both are candidate successors to GPQA Diamond in the frontier-discrimination role through 2026-Q3 and beyond. Humanity's Last Exam and FrontierMath are the next-generation discrimination benchmarks — both owned by evaluators in the tracked set (CAIS/Scale SEAL and Epoch AI). The saturation transition is the period when the question "who owns the benchmark that actually separates frontier models" becomes strategically significant. The labs already invest in making their own scores on incumbent benchmarks look good; as benchmarks rotate, the institutions that curate the successors move up the power hierarchy.

Independence. Access. Mandate. Three or four institutions have all three. Everyone else is still at the observer layer.

What resolves through 2026-H2

Three threads will clarify the eval ecosystem through 2026-H2. The first is whether the US AISI funding question resolves — if statutory authorisation passes, the institute's mandate hardens; if not, the labs' calculus on taking its findings seriously changes.7Note 7Overlaps with the AI regulation patchwork dossier spike. UK AISI and US AISI sit in both dossiers — as evaluators here (access arrangements, findings), as regulatory actors there (statutory mandate, enforcement posture). The institutions are the same; the roles are separable. The second is whether any Chinese frontier lab enters a public access arrangement with an AISI; none has through 2026-H1, and the jurisdictional asymmetry is structural. The third is whether a negative evaluator finding is published at the same time a lab ships the model that got the negative finding. That will reveal the actual mandate hierarchy faster than any amount of published methodology.
