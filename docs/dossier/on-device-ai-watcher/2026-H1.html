On-device is not a smaller cloud.

In 2026-H1, a phone-class model does real work — transcription, translation, summarization, limited coding — without leaving the device. The tier has its own rules, and three poles are already competing.

Rene Luijk · Apr 21, 2026 · 7 min read · on-device mobile privacy monitoring
TL;DR

Phone-class MMLU top scores have risen from roughly 60% a year ago to 70–75% in the current wave. Gemma 4 E-variants, Apple Intelligence Foundation Models (GA to third-party developers in iOS 19), and Android AICore reaching the Samsung S25 line have made 2026-H1 the first period where on-device AI ships as a product primitive, not a research demo.

The interesting thing is not that phones got smaller models. It is that on-device is a different market with different economics — no per-token cost, battery as the binding constraint, privacy as the default — and three poles (Apple, Google, open-weight) are already carving it into segments.

When Omar Sanseviero showed Gemma 4 running on an iPhone, a Raspberry Pi, and — via community work — a Nintendo Switch at the AI Engineer conference in April, the demo landed without hype-ceiling language. It was not framed as research. It was framed as something builders ship today.1Note 1Omar Sanseviero (Google DeepMind), AI Engineer conference, April 2026. Methodology in force for this dossier: four device tiers (flagship phone, mid-range phone, tablet/laptop, embedded) with named reference devices (iPhone 15 Pro, Pixel 9, MacBook Air M3); three model-size axes (sub-2B, 2–8B, 8B+); three benchmarks at size (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval) with noise caveats. Quarterly cadence. That framing is the story of on-device AI in 2026-H1. The capability envelope of a phone-class model — under four billion parameters, quantised, runnable on silicon you already own — has crossed thresholds that a year ago read as speculative. Phone-class MMLU top scores have risen from roughly sixty percent in early 2025 to seventy to seventy-five percent in the current wave.2Note 2Trajectory from lab-published model cards and Hugging Face leaderboard aggregators, early 2025 through April 2026. Small-model scores carry high format sensitivity — the same model can swing ten points on prompt format alone — so the 60% → 75% range is directional, not an audited number. The direction, not the decimal, is the thing. Transcription, translation, summarization, and limited coding are now viable offline on a three-year-old flagship phone. For the class of workloads that fits inside that envelope, the substrate changed.

What actually changed in twelve months

The substrate moved in three directions at once. On the model side, Google shipped Gemma 4 E-variants under Apache 2.0 with a novel architectural technique called Per-Layer Embeddings — an embedding table that works as a lookup rather than a matrix multiply, letting two billion parameters stay loaded on the GPU while the rest of the model lives in CPU memory or on disk.3Note 3Gemma 4 E2B has roughly 4B parameters total but only loads 2B into GPU memory; the per-layer embeddings can sit on disk via llama.cpp's --override-tensor flag. This is a phone-shaped design decision, not a cloud-shaped one: it accepts unified-memory constraints and trades some latency for effective capability at the phone size. Llama 3.2's 1B and 3B variants reached production maturity on Meta's ExecuTorch mobile runtime. Qwen 2.5, Phi-4 mini, Granite 3, and community distillations of DeepSeek V3 filled out the open-weight tier at comparable sizes.

On the platform side, Apple Intelligence extended its on-device Foundation Models API to third-party developers through the iOS 19 cycle, so apps no longer need to bundle their own model.4Note 4Apple Foundation Models API: developer-accessible on Apple Silicon iOS and macOS devices, backed by a ~3B on-device model plus Private Cloud Compute as the hybrid escape valve. Gated to Apple Silicon — not portable. Android's AICore reached the Samsung S25 flagship line through a System Core integration, meaning app developers can now target on-device AI across Pixel and Samsung via one API.5Note 5AICore present on Pixel 8+, Pixel 9, Pixel 10, Samsung S24, Samsung S25. Gemini Nano 2 (~3.25B class) is the default model behind it. Qualcomm Snapdragon silicon underneath — the same substrate that powers Windows Copilot+ PCs. On the silicon side, Qualcomm's Snapdragon 8 Gen 4 and Snapdragon X Elite now sit underneath both Android phones and Windows Copilot+ laptops, making NPU-accelerated inference a default rather than a premium.

Three poles, not two

The existing framing of AI markets has two poles — cloud-closed and open-weight. On-device adds a third dimension: platform-locked. Apple Intelligence runs only on Apple Silicon. Gemini Nano via AICore runs only through Android's platform APIs. Both treat on-device AI as a vertical-integration asset — silicon, OS, model, and API layer all controlled by the same entity. Neither is portable. That is not a limitation in their respective strategies; it is the strategy.

The third pole — Gemma 4 plus Llama 3.2 plus Qwen plus Phi plus the rest — is the portable open-weight stack that runs on llama.cpp, MLX, Ollama, and ExecuTorch. No OS integration, no default behind the Shortcut app or Assistant summons, no platform-level Private Cloud Compute escape valve. In exchange: it runs anywhere, the weights are open, and builders who care about portability or jurisdictional independence can actually deliver. The three poles are not competing for the same workloads. They are carving the tier into segments that correspond to who controls what.

If you want the most raw intelligence, use Gemini or your model of choice. But for on-device, open models are getting there.6Note 6Omar Sanseviero, AI Engineer conference, April 2026. A useful read because it comes from inside Google — a lab that is simultaneously shipping Gemini (closed-frontier), Gemini Nano (closed platform-integrated on-device), and Gemma 4 (open-weight on-device). Google is the only lab running all three tiers; the quote is a disarmingly honest summary of where each tier currently belongs.

Different economics, not smaller ones

The easy mistake is to treat on-device as cloud inference made cheaper. It is not. The cost axis changes: there is no per-token bill. The cost is the device, which is already a sunk cost, plus battery and thermal headroom, both of which are workload-specific and non-linear. Latency changes too. Cloud TTFT is a network round-trip plus a cold start; on-device TTFT depends on whether the model is already resident in memory and whether the NPU is warm.7Note 7Reported community benchmarks on flagship phones (iPhone 15 Pro, Pixel 9 Pro): roughly 20–40 tok/s at sub-2B quantised, 8–15 tok/s at 2–4B quantised. Cold-start to first token: ~500ms–1.5s depending on model size and runtime warmup. Platform-integrated stacks keep a structural TTFT advantage via OS-level warm-state preservation. Platform-integrated stacks (Apple Intelligence, AICore) hold a structural warm-state advantage over bundled-model apps because the OS can keep the model loaded across processes.

Privacy changes too, and not optionally. On-device inference does not leave the device by default. For workloads where that matters — legal drafting, medical summarization, private journaling, anything touching content that shouldn't be logged by a third party — the tier is not a cost optimisation. It is the only tier that meets the requirement. For workloads that don't care about privacy but care about offline viability (subways, planes, field work), the same conclusion lands from a different angle. The economic calculation a builder makes is therefore not cloud-minus-cost; it is cloud-or-on-device-for-this-workload, and the answer increasingly bifurcates by workload rather than scaling down smoothly.

What stays cloud, for now

Frontier capability doesn't fit on a phone in this cycle.8Note 8The top of GPQA Diamond (~87%) is held by Claude Opus 4.7 at ~75B+ parameters — see the Inference Economics Watcher dossier for the hosted-inference picture. Phone memory budgets in 2026 comfortably accommodate 4B models at 4-bit quantisation; 8B is laptop-territory; 31B dense is Gemma 4's largest and explicitly a laptop model per Google's own framing. The top of GPQA Diamond sits at roughly eighty-seven percent on a seventy-five-billion-parameter closed model. No phone runs that this year. Long-context workloads (a million tokens in working memory) will cross eventually, but not at current phone memory budgets. Anything requiring knowledge of events after the model's training cutoff still needs a cloud call. On-device is a tier, not a ceiling. The useful question in 2026 is which workloads have crossed the on-device threshold — not whether the whole stack has.

On-device is a tier, not a fallback. A phone that can transcribe a meeting, translate a document, and write a function — offline, in your pocket, for free per token — is not a smaller version of a datacenter. It is a different product.

What resolves through 2026-Q3

Several threads will clarify the tier before the next print. The first is whether any phone-class (sub-4B) model crosses eighty percent on MMLU or GSM8K; threshold-crossing would force the dossier to rotate to harder small-model benchmarks. The second is whether Llama 4's smallest variant ships with first-class ExecuTorch targeting — that decides whether Meta's open-weight mobile story stays competitive with Gemma 4 E-variants. The third is whether the next generation of Snapdragon silicon (X Elite 2, 8 Gen 5) produces a material NPU performance jump that changes what runs. The fourth is whether Apple Foundation Models API adoption by third-party apps produces visible category winners in translation, summarisation, and coding assistance. The fifth, and perhaps the one that resets the picture fastest, is whether the Chinese open-weight community ships phone-class Qwen 3 and DeepSeek next-gen small entries that compete on reasoning benchmarks at sub-2B size.

The capability-per-phone curve has been quiet for a long time. It is not quiet now. What is worth paying attention to is not the specific scores at specific sizes — those will keep rising. It is that three poles are already competing for different segments of a market that did not exist at this scale twelve months ago.
