Announced is easy. Energized is hard.

The AI race has a capex number and a physical-delivery number and they are not the same number. The ratio between them is the load-bearing observation for 2026.

Rene Luijk · Apr 20, 2026 · 6 min read · datacenters power capex monitoring
TL;DR

Of roughly 16 GW of US AI datacenter projects announced with 2026 opening dates, approximately 5 GW is actually under construction. The bottleneck is not demand, not GPUs, and not capital — it is transformers, switchgear, interconnect queues, and a permitting apparatus that was never designed for single-customer gigawatt-scale loads.

2026-H1 is the period where the announcements are still running ahead of the physics. The useful question is not who spent the most. It is who actually got built, energized, and chipped.

The headline numbers of the AI build-out are staggering and public. Amazon has guided roughly $200 billion of capital expenditure in 2026. Alphabet is in the $175 to $185 billion range, revised upward three times from its initial 2025 guidance. Meta is at $115 to $135 billion, Microsoft approximately $100 billion, Oracle roughly $50 billion — the largest year-over-year jump of the set. Aggregate capex across the top five hyperscalers crosses $690 billion for the year.1Note 1Figures from company 2026 guidance and Futurum Group's aggregation. The Futurum number explicitly excludes the OpenAI Stargate $500B four-year commitment — which is on top of the hyperscaler spend, not inside it.

These numbers are real. They are also not the same thing as delivered AI infrastructure. Of the roughly 140 large-scale US datacenter projects that announced 2026 opening dates — representing about 16 gigawatts of planned capacity — only about 5 GW is actually under construction as of April 2026. Two-thirds of the projects are stuck in pre-production phases and are considered unlikely to open on schedule.2Note 2Aggregated by humai.blog from 140 tracked US datacenter projects. "Under construction" means steel in the air or equivalent physical progress. The remaining two-thirds are in announced, permitting, or delayed states. This is not a marginal gap. It is the central observation of the year.

What the bottleneck actually is

Microsoft offers the clearest picture of the mechanism. As of early 2026 the company's unfulfilled Azure datacenter backlog sits at approximately $80 billion — contracts signed with customers that cannot yet be served.3Note 3Microsoft Q2 FY26 guidance and public commentary. The backlog is notably not a demand-side problem — it represents booked commercial contracts that physical capacity cannot yet deliver against. The bottleneck is not demand softness. It is not GPU supply; Azure's Nvidia and Trainium allocations are among the largest on record. It is not capital; Microsoft generated the capex internally. The specific constraint is electrical components: large power transformers, medium-voltage switchgear, grid-scale battery systems. Lead times on some transformer classes have reached five years.

This is the physics of the race. A hyperscaler can commit $100 billion in capex and still be unable to energize a campus because the transformer it needs does not exist and cannot be manufactured fast enough. The capital is abundant. The physical substrate is scarce.

The cheapest watt is the one you have already energized. The most expensive is the one you announced in a press release and cannot yet deliver.

Three archetypes trying to route around physics

The tracked operators have converged on three distinct strategies for solving the delivery problem. Each solves a different part of the bottleneck, and each implies a different failure mode when the physics bites back.

Integrated utility co-financing. Meta's Richland Parish Louisiana campus — four million square feet on 2,250 acres, $27 billion, 2 GW at full build-out — comes with a rider. Meta is co-financing 5.2 gigawatts of new natural-gas generation at Entergy Louisiana, plus roughly 240 miles of new 500-kV transmission, plus 2.5 GW of renewable additions. The Louisiana Public Service Commission fast-tracked seven new power plants for the site in April 2026.4Note 4Fox 8 Live and NOLA.com coverage of the April 15, 2026 Louisiana PSC fast-track ruling. This is the largest utility-scale co-financed generation build ever tied to a single AI customer — a structural precedent. The bet is that the utility building the plant and the hyperscaler consuming the power are the same economic actor in all but legal form.

Speed over everything. xAI's Colossus in Memphis is at 2 GW and 230,000 operational GPUs — which makes it currently the world's largest single-site AI training cluster — and that capacity is powered primarily by on-site gas turbines. Colossus 2 is energizing with a target of more than a million GPUs across 550,000 Blackwell GB200/GB300 nodes.5Note 5Introl's January 2026 reporting. The on-site gas approach sidesteps the transformer/interconnect queue but puts xAI at the front of local environmental and air-permit scrutiny — the cost of speed has a different shape, not a zero. This is the fastest-to-energized approach in the tracked set. It also puts xAI in the path of local regulatory friction that slower-and-cleaner operators do not face.

Consortium capital. Stargate — OpenAI plus Oracle plus SoftBank plus Microsoft — is attempting a third route: concurrent construction at consortium scale across multiple sites. Abilene Phase I is at 1.2 GW with a mid-2026 energization target; Phase II adds another gigawatt; five further US sites were announced in January 2026, pushing Stargate toward 10 GW aggregate. The argument is that no single hyperscaler balance sheet can absorb the transformer and power backlog alone, but a JV running many sites in parallel can.6Note 6Stargate consortium announcement, January 21 2026. The formal structure — Oracle, SoftBank, Microsoft, OpenAI — exists precisely to diversify across hyperscaler power-supply chains rather than depend on a single provider.

The nuclear cluster

The most consequential shift of Q1 2026 is that three hyperscaler nuclear arrangements now sit on the books simultaneously. Microsoft signed a 20-year, 835 MW fixed-price PPA with Constellation Energy to restart a reactor at Three Mile Island — now officially the Christopher M. Crane Clean Energy Center — with a 2027-to-2028 restart target.7Note 7Data Center Frontier coverage, September 2024 signing. First time a retired US reactor is being brought back to serve a single corporate client. Represents the template that the Amazon and Google deals followed structurally. Amazon holds a 1,920 MW offtake from Talen Energy's Susquehanna plant through 2042, now restructured into a front-of-meter arrangement that avoids the FERC rejection that blocked the prior co-location model in November 2024.8Note 8Talen Energy investor filings; RTO Insider; Power Magazine. Transmission reconfiguration scheduled for Spring 2026 concurrent with Susquehanna's refueling outage — the existing 300 MW co-located load transitions to full PPA delivery at that point. Google has partnered with Kairos Power for a 500 MW small-modular-reactor fleet targeting the early 2030s.

Cumulatively: roughly 3.25 gigawatts of carbon-free nuclear capacity is under contract for hyperscaler AI load. None of it is delivering power today. The Microsoft and Amazon reactors are existing infrastructure being restarted or reconfigured; the Google SMRs are new technology that still needs to be built. Whether any of the three is energized specifically for AI load before 2028 is the most resolvable open prediction on this monitor.

The queue behind all of it

One number captures the scale of the problem that still has to be solved. ERCOT, the Texas grid operator, ended 2025 with roughly 233 gigawatts of large-load interconnection requests in its queue — a 300 percent increase year over year, and more than 70 percent of those requests coming from datacenters.9Note 9Energy Edge Consulting analysis of ERCOT interconnection data, end-2025. 233 GW is roughly three times total ERCOT peak demand. Only a fraction of queued requests historically convert to energized load, but the composition — 70%+ datacenter — is the unusual signal. PJM reported that data center load growth was the primary driver of high capacity prices in its 2026/27 auction, and that unmet demand is plausible in coming years even if the entire generation interconnection queue is built on schedule. Both ISOs are introducing expedited interconnection tracks for large loads through 2026, and in early 2026 the US Department of Energy directed FERC to accelerate datacenter interconnection. The official response is in motion. The question is whether it catches up.

The capital is abundant. The substrate is scarce.

What to watch in 2026-H2

  1. Does Stargate Abilene Phase I hit the mid-2026 full-gigawatt energization target, or slip into 2027?
  2. Do any of the five new US Stargate sites announced in January 2026 move out of the announced state into permitting or construction by end of Q3?
  3. Does the announced-to-operational ratio — 16 GW to 5 GW in the April baseline — improve, hold, or deteriorate in the Q4 rescan?
  4. Does PJM's August 2026 Expedited Interconnection Track produce measurable queue throughput, or become another delayed regulatory artefact?
  5. First confirmation or slippage on the Microsoft Three Mile Island / Crane restart timeline. This is the cleanest proxy for whether the hyperscaler-nuclear playbook is deliverable.

The AI race in 2026 is not primarily a capability race. It is a physical-delivery race with a capital-allocation headline. Who actually gets built, energized, and chipped is the question this monitor exists to answer — one site, one PPA, one transformer lead-time at a time.
