AI and Labor Monitor · 2026-H1

Five economists, one labor market

Two recent Nobel laureates, three senior figures, all reading the same evidence — and arriving at incompatible answers. The shape of the disagreement is the signal.

Monitoring baseline · 2026-H1 Sources: peer-reviewed papers, working papers, and on-record commentary through April 2026

Two of the economists working most carefully on AI and labor have recently collected Nobel prizes: Daron Acemoglu in 2024, Philippe Aghion in 2025. Together with David Autor, Erik Brynjolfsson, and Andrew McAfee, they form something close to the front rank of the field. They are all looking at the same US labor-market data, the same productivity prints, the same published studies. And they disagree on the first-order question — whether artificial intelligence is currently producing meaningful macro-level change — by roughly an order of magnitude. Acemoglu models AI's contribution to annual productivity growth over the coming decade at around one-twentieth of one percent. Aghion, reading the same evidence, arrives at nearly a full percentage point per year. That gap is not a rounding error. It is the central observation this monitor is built to watch.

Two ranges that do not reconcile

Acemoglu's estimate, published in The Simple Macroeconomics of AI, treats current AI deployment as what he calls so-so automation — technology that cuts labor costs without meaningful productivity gain, a choice rather than a destiny. His ten-year AI contribution works out to roughly 0.55 to 0.71 percentage points of total-factor-productivity growth cumulative, or about 0.05 percent annually. Aghion and Bunel, working from a task-based model for the San Francisco Fed, estimate AI's contribution at 0.68 to 1.3 percentage points per year. The numbers are not measuring slightly different things. They are measuring the same thing and coming to conclusions that cannot both be true.

Brynjolfsson sits between the two but leans toward the optimistic read, on the grounds that 2025 US total-factor-productivity growth printed at approximately 2.7 percent — roughly double the decade average — and that the intangible-investment J-curve he and Rock have been publishing on for years is now showing its harvest phase. Autor does not pick a productivity number, but reads the 16.7 percent wage growth in the AI-exposed computer-systems-design sector since fall 2022, against 7.5 percent nationally, as evidence that AI is already reshaping the returns to expertise. McAfee reads the same evidence and remains unconvinced that any macro signal has cleared the noise floor.

The question nobody wants to answer directly

Underneath the productivity argument is a question about the middle class. The forty-year trend in the US and most high-income economies has been the hollowing-out of routine, middle-skill occupations — ATM operators, travel agents, middle-manager administrative roles — with work polarising toward the high-skill professional class and low-wage service employment. Autor's reading of AI is that it reverses the polarisation: where previous automation replaced the routine parts of middle-skill work, AI augments the judgment parts of high-skill work, effectively democratising expertise that was previously gated behind years of training. If he is right, the middle class comes back. If Acemoglu is right about the direction AI is currently taking — what he and Autor and Johnson recently called, jointly, the commodification of expertise — the middle class gets further dismantled, now from the top.

Both readings are defensible with today's data. Both imply different policy regimes. Both cannot be simultaneously correct.

The two-sided squeeze

The most specific empirical claim in Brynjolfsson's recent work is not about aggregate productivity. It is about a cohort: employment among twenty-two- to twenty-five-year-olds in the most AI-exposed white-collar occupations fell about thirteen percent on a relative basis through 2025. Replicated by the Dallas Fed in February 2026. From the bottom, a separate finding — minimum-wage increases in manufacturing now predict faster industrial-robot adoption — shows the same pressure arriving at the other end of the wage distribution through different machinery. Whatever AI is doing to productivity, it is doing something specific to the entry-level job ladder, from both sides.

We currently have the wrong direction for AI. We're using it too much for automation and not enough for providing expertise and information to workers. — Daron Acemoglu, MIT Technology Review, 2025

The quote is exactly the part of Acemoglu's position that does not show up in the productivity numbers. His macro estimate is small because he thinks current AI is being pointed at the wrong task category. A different AI — one optimised for amplifying worker judgment rather than replacing worker cost — could in principle produce macro numbers closer to Aghion's. The difference between the two economists is less empirical than it is about which world they are forecasting into.

The institution axis

Which brings in the question McAfee and Brynjolfsson spend the least time on and Acemoglu, Aghion, and Autor spend the most: institutions. Aghion's reference design is the Danish flexicurity model — flexible firing, ninety percent wage replacement for up to two years, funded state retraining. Autor has flagged wage insurance as the most promising US policy instrument for workers displaced by AI. Acemoglu's framework requires, explicitly, both regulation and new institutions to redirect innovation toward pro-worker patterns. Four of the five tracked economists place institutional choice — not technology trajectory — as the primary determinant of how this decade ends. McAfee's position emphasises firm-level task unbundling. It is the lightest-institution reading in the set.

This axis is where the monitor will show movement first. Technology forecasts can stay fuzzy for years. A named national wage-insurance program explicitly motivated by AI displacement — which is the fourth open prediction in the underlying document — would resolve cleanly in one direction or the other.

What is actually useful here

The question a reader arrives with — who is right? — turns out to be the wrong question in 2026. It is April, the 2026 H1 productivity prints have not landed, cohort data for the critical twenty-two to twenty-five bracket is still being updated half-yearly, and no major economy has yet passed policy that identifies AI as a named cause of displacement. There is not enough resolution in the evidence to close the Acemoglu-Aghion gap or decide between Autor's and the hollowing-out precedent. There is, however, enough structure to know what would close the gap: specific productivity prints, specific cohort movements, a specific policy passing. The monitor exists to watch those signals land in real time against the priors that have been publicly declared.

What to watch in 2026-H2

  1. H1 2026 US total-factor-productivity prints — does the 2.7 percent 2025 level hold, or retrace toward the pre-2024 baseline? A hold shifts the Brynjolfsson-Aghion reading; a retrace favours Acemoglu.
  2. Cohort employment data for twenty-two- to twenty-five-year-olds in AI-exposed occupations — continues falling, stabilises, or recovers. The most resolvable of the four open predictions.
  3. OECD-level policy activity on AI-motivated wage insurance. Any named national program passing by end of 2027 confirms Aghion and Autor; none confirms the no-institutional-response path.
  4. Late-2026 publications from Acemoglu and Aghion's teams — whether their productivity estimates narrow toward each other as more evidence accumulates, or stay an order of magnitude apart.
  5. Wage-dispersion data within AI-exposed occupations: if the top-decile-to-median ratio rises, Autor's expertise-premium reading gains; if compression appears, Acemoglu's commodification-of-expertise reading gains.

Five economists, one labor market. The gap between them is not noise. It is a genuinely open question, and the evidence that will close it is beginning to arrive.
