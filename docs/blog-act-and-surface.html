Act and surface.

The registry has been answering two different questions under one umbrella. I only saw it because I was comparing it to something else.

Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedMay 1, 2026 Read5 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-act-and-surface.html

The detour was Gemini CLI's experimental Task Tracker. PR #24556 ships a small DAG of tasks with parentId and dependencies[] — session-local working memory for an agent's execution plan. One entity, three sub-types, four statuses. The schema is tiny on purpose. No semantic contracts; the agent just decomposes a goal into ordered work.

I went in to compare it against our 42 convergence types. There's an obvious-looking integration shape — a tracker task carries metadata.convergenceType = "decision-record", the agent reads the registry's promptGuidance when picking up the task, the harness travels with the work. Not the point. I wasn't trying to ship that.

The point was the lens. Holding our registry next to a much smaller, much tighter schema made something in our own vocabulary visible that I'd been walking past for months: not all 42 of our types are the same kind of thing.

The two questions

Look at any convergence type and ask: when I'm filling in a card here, am I recording a thinking-action I just performed, or am I surfacing state that exists somewhere else?

TypeWhat a card isKind
decision-recorda decision I just madeact
capability-surfacewhether each capability is wired or notsurface
attempt-logan attempt I just triedact
expert-stance-trackwhat an external thinker has said over timesurface
proof-ladderverification work I just didact
status-snapshotthe current shape of the docsurface

These two questions felt like one question for a long time. They're both about "what kind of thing is this section holding?" They're not. One is about authorship of the cards; the other is about reflection of state.

If I stopped maintaining this section, would the underlying state still change in the world?

— the disambiguating test

Surface, if yes. Act, if the recorded thinking would just stop accumulating.

Why it matters now

The smallest reason: it sharpens the existing registry. Every reader was already recovering this distinction in their head. Naming it once puts the work in one place instead of forty-two.

The middle reason: /convergence-advisor was failing at this question. When someone proposed a new type, the skill asked about sources, projection, derivation — but never about kind. So new types entered the registry already confused.

The biggest reason: it's the difference between two timescales the registry has been pretending are one. Surface types are durable substrate — they hold across sessions, they outlive any particular pass of work. Act types are working memory of thought — they belong to a specific stretch of doing, and they accumulate or close. Mixing them quietly cost me clarity every time I edited a doc.

The cost I haven't paid yet

Promoting kind to a label is the cheap version. The expensive version is making it operational — composition rules ("a proof-ladder parent only allows verification-checkpoint children"), closure predicates ("an attempt-log closes regardless of outcome; a decision-record only closes when its description is a single declarative statement"), edge semantics ("X depends-on Y means X uses Y as evidence vs. X verifies Y").

The expensive version is the move from registry-as-language to registry-as-type-system. It would prevent the failure class the harness essay named — thinking-discipline drift, polished but level-mixed output — but it would break the property that made the registry pleasant to extend. Right now adding a new convergence type is a JSON edit. With operational types, every addition is a code-and-tests change.

I'm not paying that cost today. Today's commit is the label and three downstream surfaces: the renderer shows the kind in each section header, the compositor GUI shows it on each registry card, and the convergence-advisor asks the kind question first.

The borderlines I had to write down

Most types are obviously one or the other. A handful aren't:

  • experiment-evidence-surface — name says surface, behavior says act. Each card is an experiment performed and the evidence it produced.
  • attempt-log and strategic-move-log — both logs, different kind. Attempt-log records my actions (act). Strategic-move-log tracks external actors' moves (surface).
  • design-implementation-alignment — interpretive work to find the alignment, but the cards are pairs that exist in the world. Surface.
  • theme-thread-map, scene-dependency-map — interpretive mapping looks like surfacing existing structure, but the value is in the act of tracing. Act.

Borderlines are where the discipline shows. Anyone can label the obvious ones.

What this is, and isn't

This isn't preparation for an integration. The Gemini comparison was useful as a mirror, not a roadmap. Other people's design choices — especially deliberately small ones — are good for catching what your own design has been quietly hiding.

The registry is sharper today than it was yesterday because the comparison forced the question. That's the whole gain. If something downstream ends up using the act subset later, fine; if nothing does, the registry is still better.
