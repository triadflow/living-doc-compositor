Taste, made into a surface.

Four small convergence types for a designer working a client system without losing themselves to it.

Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedMay 4, 2026 Read5 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-design-system-types.en.html

Two pressures pull a designer in opposite directions when they take on a client. Be yourself — your idiom is what the client hired. Serve the brief — make this system answer this client's constraints. Most designers carry both unwritten, in their heads. The cost of unwritten is that the trade-offs get buried. Naming them, separately, is cheap — and it changes how the work shows up.

This week I added four convergence types to the registry for that tension. Not because the registry was lacking — the designer-versus-brief problem has been nameable and separable for a long time. It just happened to be design's turn.

What recurs is data

The first type is taste-signature. The section lists recurring traits across the prior systems the designer has shipped. Each card is one trait — warm grays over neutral, a soft small-radius motif, generous spacing scales — with pointers to the prior systems where the trait appears.

The discipline: a recurring trait needs at least two prior-system pointers. One is anecdotal. The rule turns "I have a taste" into "here is taste, with evidence." It also names the thing nobody else can see — the throughline across a designer's body of work, the part of any system that survived the brief.

A recurring trait needs at least two prior-system pointers. One is anecdotal.

— the rule that turns taste into evidence

The brief deserves a paired move

The second type is brief-to-system-alignment. Each card pairs one constraint with one response, plus rationale. "Premium feel" → "1.5× spacing scale." "No neon" → "chroma capped at 60." "Exceptionally accessible" → "AAA contrast on body text."

The discipline: a card with only a constraint is a question. A card with only a response is a decision someone made for some reason that isn't here. The pairing forces explicit translation. The state field — aligned, partial, deferred, conflicting — is honest about what's resolved and what isn't. Partial and conflicting aren't failure; they mean the system and the brief have a tension, named.

What you carry is named

The third type is design-system-derivation. The cards record what carries over, with explicit moves: kept, swapped, inverted, scaled, dropped. "Kept the 1.25 type scale from Studio Lin." "Swapped the 8px radius for 12px — 8 reads a bit studio-y, 12 lands premium." "Inverted: dark mode is primary this time." "Dropped Atelier's hairline borders — they don't survive AAA contrast."

The discipline: every card has a prior-system pointer. Anonymous influence isn't derivation. And kept moves still need rationale — kept without rationale loses the data the move was built on.

Pointers, not copies

The fourth type is design-system-surface. Each card is one primitive — a token, a component, a motif — pointing at where the primitive actually lives. Figma variable. tokens.json key. CSS custom property. Code repo path. The card never owns the value. Status reflects lifecycle: proposed → in-figma → coded → shipped → deprecated.

The discipline: pointers, not copies. The card is a navigable reference; the truth is in the canonical source.

What the four sections look like as one doc: a designer's template showing Mara Lin's taste-signature across three prior systems alongside one active client engagement — Alto Audio, a consumer audio brand. Same abstract types, made concrete.

Not just for designers

This shape isn't designer-specific. The same separation — what recurs across my prior work, what this engagement asks, what I carried over, what I'm building — turns up in any field of complex thinking. Researchers, architects, lawyers, writers all live with it. Designers happen to be the field where I built the four types this week.

Two of the new types — taste-signature and design-system-surface — are surface. The traits and primitives exist regardless of whether the section is maintained. The other two — brief-to-system-alignment and design-system-derivation — are act. The recorded thinking would simply stop accumulating if the section were abandoned. That distinction was the subject of an earlier note, Act and surface; these four types are its first concrete application to a new domain.

Naming the kinds doesn't make the work shorter. It makes the work more honest about what it is.
