The registry is a language.

Complex work stalls on seeing the same structure together, not on capacity to do. A shared semantic language is what lets many minds — and increasingly, many agents — operate from the same picture. The test of a living language is whether a new word can enter the dictionary without breaking what's already being spoken.

RL
Rene Luijk Triadflow
PublishedApr 19, 2026 Read10 min StatusLive Filedocs/blog-registry-is-a-language.html

There is a common failure mode in complex work, and it does not depend on the kind of work. Writing a long book. Proving a trading model. Reshaping a team's process. Hardening a system after an incident. Triaging a bug nobody has cracked. In all of these, the bottleneck is rarely capacity to act. It is the cost of getting everyone working on the thing to see the same structure.

Each participant reads the sources — the draft, the code, the invariants, the design files, the ticket thread — and forms a picture in their head. The pictures are similar. They are not the same. Decisions get revisited because nobody can confirm they were decided. Facets of the objective go unattended because nobody can see whose section carries them. Parallel work diverges quietly and only becomes a problem after it is expensive to untangle. That is what stalls a complex project. Doing the thing is often the smaller half of the work.

You solve this by giving everyone a shared projection. Not a shared screen — a shared semantic projection, where every piece of content on the page has a declared shape. Where "decision record" means the same thing whether you are reading it in a book project, a quant-model project, or a security-hardening project. Where "capability surface" always has rows of things you can do and columns of whether they exist, no matter what the things are. Where the vocabulary itself carries contract, so that looking at the structure is already most of the understanding.

That is what the living doc compositor is for. Its deep commitment is not that documents look nice, or that they pull sources into one place. It is that the vocabulary — the registry of what kinds of content exist — is a language. Stable, shared, growing, and not owned by the people who maintain the tool.

Thirty-two words

Today the registry has thirty-two convergence types. Each one is an entry in a JSON file. An entry is a small semantic contract, not a schema. Fields are declared, yes, but so is what the type is for, what it is not for, which fields should stay distinct from each other, how an author should update it, how an agent should read it. A type's declaration carries about the same weight as a dictionary entry for a word: spelling, usage, grammatical role, two or three sentences about what it means and when to use it.

Some of these words you would expect: decision-record, capability-surface, verification-surface, status-snapshot. Some are more specific: formal-model, protocol-conformance, operating-surface, proof-ladder. A few are recent: symptom-observation, code-anchor, maintainer-stance, added for a class of work the existing vocabulary couldn't cleanly hold.

The point is not the list. It is that in dozens of docs across domains that have almost nothing in common — a book in progress called Tackling Complexity, a quantitative market-structure substrate, an identity-hardening initiative, a parser pipeline, an open-source issue trial — the same words keep carrying weight. A status-snapshot at the top of a book-writing doc does the same work as a status-snapshot at the top of a security-phases doc. A decision-record in a research project reads the same way as a decision-record in an operational process. A reader — human or agent — does not have to relearn what each word means when they open a new doc. The contract travels with the word, which is also why other people's living docs become so revealing.

A coordination surface, not a doc format

For most of software's history, coordination was a human problem. A team reading the same docs, attending the same meetings, converging on the same tickets. The docs were prose. The convergence happened in heads.

That model holds as long as the group is small and slow enough to keep its heads synchronized. It strains when there are more minds, moving faster — which is what agents are. Five Claude Code sessions running on the same project are five minds. Tomorrow it is fifty. Each individually capable of deep work. None of them with a shared picture of the structure of that work, unless something is carrying the picture for them.

Prose cannot carry the picture. Prose is expensive to re-read, easy to misinterpret, and drifts silently. You also cannot test whether an action is consistent with a prose commitment. You can test whether a patch violates a typed invariant. You can check whether the card an agent is editing lives in the section the coverage edge says carries that facet. You can refuse a suggestion that drops a field the type says must stay distinct. These are checks prose does not allow.

Coordination does not scale with more intelligence. It scales with a shared language that grows as the work does.

— The bet

A typed semantic language is what makes those checks possible. Which is why the registry matters more, not less, as capability grows. It is the arbitration surface. The place agreement lives, so participants can operate from it without having to re-derive it on every pass.

The test of a living language

A language is only a language if new words can enter it without being taught to every speaker. The grammar has to already know how to receive a new word. The speakers — readers, writers, listeners — have to just pick it up.

The test on the compositor side is very specific. When a shape of work shows up that the existing types don't cover, can someone add a word to the dictionary? And when they do, does every surface of the tool immediately know how to read it? The renderer. The compositor UI where you edit the doc. The Cmd+K palette that runs agent actions on a card. The mobile companion that tracks freshness. The AI prompt that generates the prose inside a card of that type.

The most recent test of that property was a bug-triage trial. Running the compositor on Textual issue #5380 — a real open-source bug with maintainer pushback, a workaround shipped in another repo, and four related issues orbiting it — revealed that the vocabulary was being stretched. Reports of symptoms with repro steps were landing in capability-surface. Attempted fixes were landing in decision-record. Maintainer positions were landing in whichever section happened to have space. On the page it looked coherent. In practice, a reader scanning the doc could not tell, before reading a card, what kind of thing it was. The type pill no longer carried weight.

The response was to add five words to the dictionary: symptom-observation, code-anchor, attempt-log, issue-orbit, maintainer-stance. Each declared its own small contract — what fields it carries, what status values make sense, what it is for, what it is decidedly not for, how to write one. Adding the five was an edit to one file — no deploy, no renderer change, no rewrite of any skill.

The doc was recomposed the same afternoon. The language was five words richer. The compositor's type picker listed them. The AI-assist knew how to write and edit them. The palette offered type-specific actions for each. The day after, the book project could have used one of the new types if the next chapter happened to need it — the extension landed for every speaker, immediately, because the dictionary was the thing that changed.

The rule that keeps it alive

This property only holds under one commitment. It is written in the repository's contributor notes, one line, and everything else rests on it:

The registry is the single source of truth for convergence types. Never hard-code type knowledge in the renderer or compositor.

The renderer does not have a branch for code-anchor. It doesn't know code-anchor exists until it reads the registry. What it knows is: how to draw a card-grid when the registry tells it a type's projection is card-grid, how to render status pills when the registry names the status set, how to place icons that the registry declares as SVG paths. Generic grammar, specific vocabulary.

Break that rule once — one if (type === 'symptom-observation') somewhere in the rendering path — and the property quietly dissolves. The language stops being a language, because new words suddenly require teaching each speaker what to do with them. The cost of the next extension rises. So does the cost of the one after. Slowly the tool turns back into a configuration: a fixed set of behaviors the user tunes, rather than a vocabulary the user speaks.

Stretching is how a language dies

The counterfactual for the #5380 case was cheaper on the day. Keep three existing types, spread five content shapes across them, ship. Zero registry entries. Zero new status sets. Zero new icons. Why not?

Because the cost doesn't show up on the day. It shows up a week later, when a second reader — or a different agent pass, or a downstream check — tries to rely on the words meaning what they say. A capability-surface section holding attempt logs is no longer a capability surface. It's whatever its contents happen to be this week. Readers have to re-derive the category from each card. Contracts the type was supposed to carry have faded. The vocabulary has quietly lost a word to overload.

The lens Semantic stability, not volume. The real tradeoff when the vocabulary needs extending is not "how many types is too many." It is whether each word preserves a distinct contract a reader can trust. Add a word when the shape genuinely differs. Stretch an existing word only when the shape genuinely matches. Stretching is cheap only if you never need the meaning to hold.

Who the language belongs to

The most important consequence of "the registry is the single source of truth" comes down to who can extend the language, not to architecture.

If extending the vocabulary is a code change — a new handler, a new branch, a new deploy — then the language belongs to whoever ships code. The author of a book cannot add a word. A researcher running a new class of experiment cannot. An operations lead who just ran their third incident of a recognizable shape cannot. Neither can an agent that has noticed a pattern it has no name for. The vocabulary's growth is throttled by the distance between noticing a shape and knowing how to merge a pull request.

If extending the vocabulary is a dictionary edit, the language belongs to whoever is paying attention to the work. That is what changes. The book author can sit with their draft, notice that certain chapters share a shape the existing types don't quite capture, write a crisp contract for what the shape is and is not, and add the word. The next writer to use the compositor inherits it. Whoever is closest to the texture of the work is the one who gets to name it.

This is the quiet wager of the project: that over the coming years, more and more of the language will be named by people who are not the tool's maintainers. An ops lead who added incident-arc. A therapist who added a type for parts-work notes that hold their shape across sessions. A music researcher who added one for the sonic-fingerprint structure that keeps emerging. A team lead who added one for the status-of-a-standing-initiative shape that prose was never any good at. The substrate grows with the work, named by whoever is doing it.

A layer below governance

A previous piece here was about the doc-level substrate: objective facets, coverage edges, invariants, the structural fingerprint that makes staleness loud. That layer handles commitments a specific doc makes — this doc's objective decomposes into these parts, these sections carry them, these rules hold.

The registry is one layer beneath that. Before you author any doc, the substrate has already declared what shapes of content exist and what each shape means. You pick from the vocabulary. Governance is what you then do with the vocabulary for this doc. The two layers compose: a typed doc with typed governance is something both humans and agents can reason over. A stretched doc with declared governance is something that points confidently at the wrong things.

Both layers share the same underlying move: promote implicit reasoning to explicit data. In the governance layer, the implicit reasoning was "which section carries which part of the objective." In the registry, it is "what kind of content is this and what contract does it carry." Both stop being re-derived on every pass. Both become things downstream surfaces can consume without having to ask the author what they meant.

Try it

If you have a work surface you are trying to keep coherent — a draft, a system, a running process, a messy investigation — and there is a section where you keep mentally re-categorizing the pieces every time you scan them, that is the signal. The shape you keep half-seeing is a word that does not exist in your vocabulary yet.

Open the registry. Read the thirty-two entries. If none of them quite match, propose a new one. Name what the cards carry. Name what the type is not for. Write a short operating thesis a reader or an agent could use to know when to reach for the word. Save the file. Render the doc.

Nothing else needs to change. The tool will have learned a new word, and will speak it the next time you open a doc — in your project, and in every other living doc that ever needs the shape.

That is the whole bet. Coordination does not scale with more intelligence. It scales with a shared language that grows as the work does. The registry is a commitment that that language belongs to the people doing the work. The test of its aliveness is simple: can a new word land without breaking anything? When the answer stays yes, the language is working.
